Discipleship
What it actually means to follow Jesus
Guide Note — Purpose

This study answers the question "What does it mean to be a Christian?" — not the cultural version, but the biblical one. Many people will assume being a Christian means believing certain things or living a generally moral life. Jesus' definition is far more demanding than that.

The goal is not to discourage, but to clarify. A person who understands the cost and says yes is far more prepared than one who wanders in without knowing what they signed up for.

What Is a Disciple?

The word "Christian" appears only three times in the New Testament. "Disciple" appears nearly 300 times. The early church understood the term — a learner who follows a teacher so closely that their life begins to look like the teacher's life. Not just someone who agrees with Jesus. Someone who follows him.

"If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me. For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will save it."
Luke 9:23–24
Three Requirements — Luke 9:23

Deny yourself. Take up your cross daily. Follow me.

Deny yourself
Stop being the center of your own story. Your agenda, preferences, and comfort are no longer the deciding factor.
Take up your cross
Accept suffering as part of following. A cross is not an inconvenience — it is an instrument of death to self.
Daily
This is not a one-time decision. It is a daily posture — renewed every morning, not just at conversion.
Follow me
Active obedience — going where Jesus goes, doing what Jesus does, becoming what Jesus is.
Guide Note — The Daily Question

The word "daily" is often glossed over. Press on it: "Jesus says take up your cross daily. Not once at an altar call. Not when you feel like it. Daily. What would that actually look like in your life tomorrow morning?"

Watch for the person who intellectually agrees but has never counted the cost. Agreement without surrender is not discipleship — it's religious opinion.

The Great Commission

"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you."
Matthew 28:19–20

Jesus' final command to his followers was not "make converts" or "get people to pray a prayer." It was make disciples — people who observe all that he commanded. The standard is not agreement. It is obedience.

What Discipleship Costs

"So therefore, any one of you who does not renounce all that he has does not qualify as my disciple."
Luke 14:33
Your priorities

Jesus moves to the center. Career, comfort, and reputation serve the mission — not the other way around.

Your self-determination

"Lord" means master. A disciple doesn't negotiate which commands to keep.

Your reputation

"If they persecuted me, they will also persecute you" (John 15:20). Following Jesus has social consequences.

Guide Note — Closing Well

End with a direct question: "Based on what Jesus says here, would you say you're currently living as his disciple — or as someone who finds him interesting?" Don't soften it. This is the question the study exists to ask.

If they push back — "this seems extreme" — that's the right response to encounter. Say: "It is extreme. That's Jesus' word, not mine. The question is whether he's right."

The Challenge

This Week

Read Luke 9:23–25 every morning this week. Ask yourself honestly: In what specific area of my life am I not yet denying myself and following Jesus? Write it down. Come ready to share it.

Word Study
Disciple · Follow
What the original words reveal about what Jesus is asking
μαθητής
mathētēs
noun, masculine — Greek
Disciple, learner, student. From manthanō — to learn by practice or experience, not just by hearing. The word implies an apprenticeship relationship: the disciple imitates the master.
A mathētēs in the ancient world didn't just attend the teacher's lectures. He lived with the teacher, watched how he ate, prayed, handled conflict, and treated the poor. The goal was not information — it was transformation into the teacher's image.
ἀκολουθέω
akoloutheō
verb — Greek
To follow, to accompany, to be a follower of. Implies movement — going in the same direction, at the same pace, toward the same destination. Used 90 times in the Gospels for following Jesus.
"Follow me" was Jesus' most common call. It was not an invitation to agree with his teachings. It was an invitation to physically go where he went — which meant leaving behind what you were doing before (Matthew 4:20: "immediately they left their nets and followed him").
ἀρνέομαι
arneomai
verb — Greek
To deny, to disown, to renounce. The same word used for Peter denying Jesus (Matthew 26:70). Jesus says a disciple must deny themselves — the same absolute, decisive act.
The parallel is intentional and sobering. Peter's denial of Jesus is held up as a failure of discipleship. Jesus asks his followers to turn that same decisive act on themselves — to disown self-sovereignty the way Peter disowned knowing Christ.
