First Principles · Study 4
Sin
The honest diagnosis before the cure can mean anything
Guide Note — Purpose

This is the most personal study in the series. The goal is not to make someone feel guilty — it's to help them see themselves clearly in the mirror of Scripture. Without a real understanding of sin, the cross is just a historical event and repentance is just a mood shift.

You will need to have established trust before this conversation. Share your own story — your own sins, not in graphic detail, but honestly. "I had to look at these lists and name specific things. That's what this study calls for."

If someone becomes defensive, don't press. Slow down. The Spirit does the convicting. Your job is to open the word, not to argue someone into confession.

The Universal Diagnosis

"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."
Romans 3:23

The word "all" leaves no exceptions. Religious background, good intentions, community standing — none of these change the diagnosis. Every person who has ever lived, except Jesus, has sinned.

The Consequence

"For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord." — Romans 6:23

Guide Note — What Sin Actually Is

Most people think of sin as "bad things" — lying, cheating, murder. But the Greek word hamartia means missing the mark. The mark is God's character and glory. Every time we fall short of what God designed us to be, that's sin. This is broader and more personal than a list of bad behaviors.

Ask: "When you think about sin, what comes to mind? Is it mostly things other people do, or things you do?" This question surfaces whether they've personally owned the diagnosis.

James 4:17 adds another dimension: "Whoever knows the right thing to do and fails to do it, for him it is sin." Sins of omission — not just things we've done, but things we've failed to do — count. This broadens the conversation for people who see themselves as "not that bad."

Sin Is Personal

The lists in Scripture are not meant to be read clinically. They are meant to be read personally — with a specific name attached to each one. The Sin Reference tab contains six such lists with the full Greek definitions of every sin named.

"Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these."
Galatians 5:19–21
"For from within, out of the heart of man, come evil thoughts, sexual immorality, theft, murder, adultery, coveting, wickedness, deceit, sensuality, envy, slander, pride, foolishness."
Mark 7:21–22

Notice where Jesus locates the source: the heart. Not environment. Not upbringing. Not society. The problem is internal. This is why external moral improvement can't solve it.

Guide Note — Using the Sin Lists

Go through the Sin Reference tab slowly together. For each sin, first establish what it actually means (the Greek definition), then ask: "Is this something you've struggled with? Have you seen this in your own life?" Don't rush. Share your own examples honestly.

The person needs to leave this study with specific sins they've named, not a general sense of being "imperfect." Specificity is necessary for real repentance. General guilt produces general remorse. Specific naming produces specific repentance.

Some people are surprised by the breadth of these lists — sins of the heart (pride, envy, covetousness) alongside sins of action. This is usually the moment the conversation shifts from "I'm not that bad" to "this is more serious than I thought."

The 2 Timothy 3 list is particularly useful because it describes people who have "a form of godliness but deny its power" — religious-looking people whose hearts are unchanged. For someone with a church background who may not see themselves as a sinner, this list can be eye-opening.

The Separation Sin Creates

"But your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear."
Isaiah 59:2

Sin doesn't just produce guilt. It creates distance from God — a real, relational rupture. The seeking we talked about in Study 1 is frustrated by sin we haven't honestly faced. You cannot draw close to a holy God while holding on to the things that separate you from him.

Guilt
The internal awareness that we have done wrong — the conscience functioning as designed. Not something to suppress or medicate, but something to act on.
Separation
The relational distance between us and God that sin creates. Isaiah 59:2 — God doesn't disappear; sin hides his face. The problem is on our side, not his.
Death
Romans 6:23 — the wages of sin is death. Not just physical death, but spiritual death: ongoing existence cut off from God, the source of life. This is the consequence the cross addresses.
Guide Note — The Separation

For someone who says "I don't feel particularly separated from God," ask: "When you pray, do you feel like God hears you? Do you have a real sense of his presence?" Many people describe a vague spiritual longing — something missing — without connecting it to sin. Isaiah 59:2 makes the connection explicit.

Don't be afraid to name the word "death." Romans 6:23 is direct. The consequence of sin is not just that life is harder — it's that we are moving toward death. This is what makes the cross not optional but necessary.

Sin Reference
The Lists
Greek definitions of sins named in Scripture — for honest self-examination

These lists are not meant to be read quickly or academically. Read each entry slowly. Ask honestly: Is this me? The specificity is the point — vague guilt produces vague repentance. Naming specific sins is what makes real change possible.

Six passages are included: Galatians 5, Colossians 3, 2 Timothy 3, Mark 7, Ephesians 5, and 1 Corinthians 6. Together they cover sins of action, sins of the heart, sins of speech, and sins of omission.

Word Study
Sin · Flesh · Death
What the original words reveal about sin's nature
ἁμαρτία
hamartia
noun, feminine — Greek
Sin; missing the mark. The general New Testament word for sin. From a root meaning to miss the target — originally an archery term. The mark is God's character and glory: the standard we were made to hit.
The most common NT word for sin. Not primarily about rule-breaking — about falling short of what God designed us to be. This makes sin both universal (everyone misses) and personal (each person's miss is specific to them). Romans 3:23 uses this word: "all have sinned (hēmarton) and fall short of the glory of God."
παράπτωμα
paraptōma
noun, neuter — Greek
Trespass; transgression; a false step. From para (beside, off) + piptō (to fall). The image is of stepping off the right path — deviation from the way you were meant to walk.
Used in Ephesians 2:1 — "dead in your trespasses and sins." Not just bad choices, but a fundamental wrong direction. The person who is trespassing isn't just lost — they are in territory they were never meant to be in. The word carries the idea of a stumble that becomes a trajectory.
חָטָא
chata
verb — Hebrew
To sin, to miss, to go wrong. The primary Old Testament word for sin — the same concept as hamartia: missing the mark, failing to hit the target. Notably, the same word is used to describe Saul's army missing stones with slings (Judges 20:16).
The word's original use is instructive: sin is not always deliberate rebellion. Sometimes it is simply missing what we were aimed at — falling short of the design. Both kinds count. Both matter to God. The diagnosis is the same regardless of intent.
σάρξ
sarx
noun, feminine — Greek
Flesh; the sinful nature. In Paul's usage, more than the physical body — the whole human orientation away from God: desires, instincts, appetites, and reasoning that has not been submitted to God's Spirit.
Galatians 5:19 — "the works of the flesh." This is why willpower alone can't fix the sin problem: the flesh is not just behavior, it is orientation. The whole self is turned the wrong direction. What is needed is not better behavior management but a transformation of what we are turned toward — which is what conversion is.
עָוֺן
avon
noun, masculine — Hebrew
Iniquity; guilt; the consequence of sin. From a root meaning to be bent or twisted. Captures the distortion that sin produces — not just the act, but the guilt and the twisted condition it leaves behind.
Isaiah 59:2 uses this word: "your iniquities (avonot) have made a separation between you and your God." The separation is not just positional — it is the result of a cumulative bending away from God. Isaiah 53:5 uses the same word for what Jesus bore: "he was crushed for our iniquities." The weight he carried had a name.

Key Passages
The biblical foundation for the diagnosis of sin and its consequences
The Universal Diagnosis
"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."
Romans 3:23"All" — no exceptions based on religion, upbringing, or good intentions.
"For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."
Romans 6:23Wages are earned. Death is what sin earns. The gift comes from somewhere else entirely.
"None is righteous, no, not one; no one understands; no one seeks for God. All have turned aside; together they have become worthless; no one does good, not even one."
Romans 3:10–12Paul quoting Psalms. The diagnosis is total and ancient — this is not a new problem.
The Source: The Heart
"For from within, out of the heart of man, come evil thoughts, sexual immorality, theft, murder, adultery, coveting, wickedness, deceit, sensuality, envy, slander, pride, foolishness. All these evil things come from within, and they defile a person."
Mark 7:21–23Jesus locates the source. External reform — better environment, better habits — misses it.
"The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?"
Jeremiah 17:9The heart cannot be trusted to assess itself accurately. We need an outside standard.
The Separation Sin Creates
"But your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear."
Isaiah 59:2Not God's absence — our separation. The problem is on our side, not his.
"And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked, following the course of this world."
Ephesians 2:1–2Death in sin is not a metaphor Paul is afraid to use. It is the condition before Christ.
Sins of Omission
"So whoever knows the right thing to do and fails to do it, for him it is sin."
James 4:17Sin is not only what we do. It is what we fail to do when we know better.
