Sin
The honest diagnosis before the cure can mean anything
Guide Note — Purpose

This is the most personal study in the series. The goal is not to make someone feel guilty — it's to help them see themselves clearly in the mirror of Scripture. Without a real understanding of sin, the cross is just a historical event and repentance is just a mood shift.

You will need to have established trust before this conversation. Share your own story — your own sins, not in graphic detail, but honestly. "I had to look at these lists and name specific things. That's what this study calls for."

If someone becomes defensive, don't press. Slow down. The Spirit does the convicting. Your job is to open the word, not to argue someone into confession.

The Universal Diagnosis

"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."
Romans 3:23

The word "all" leaves no exceptions. Religious background, good intentions, community standing, Southern manners — none of these change the diagnosis. Every person who has ever lived, except Jesus, has sinned.

The Consequence

"For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord." — Romans 6:23

Guide Note — What Sin Actually Is

Most people think of sin as "bad things" — lying, cheating, murder. But the Greek word hamartia means missing the mark. The mark is God's character and God's glory. Every time we fall short of what God designed us to be, that's sin. This is broader and more personal than a list of bad behaviors.

Ask: "When you think about sin, what comes to mind? Is it mostly things other people do, or things you do?" This question surfaces whether they've personally owned the diagnosis.

Sin Is Personal

The lists in Scripture are not meant to be read clinically. They are meant to be read personally — with a specific name attached to each one.

"Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these."
Galatians 5:19–21

See the Sin Reference tab for the full Greek definitions of each sin across Galatians 5, Colossians 3, 2 Timothy 3, and Ephesians 5.

Guide Note — Using the Sin Lists

Go through the lists slowly. For each sin: "What does this word actually mean?" Then: "Is this something you've struggled with?" Don't rush. Share your own examples. This is the most important and most uncomfortable part of the series — give it space.

The person needs to leave this study with specific sins they've named, not a general sense of being "imperfect." Specificity is necessary for real repentance.

The Separation Sin Creates

"But your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear."
Isaiah 59:2

Sin doesn't just produce guilt. It creates distance from God — a real, relational rupture. The seeking we talked about in Study 1 is frustrated by sin we haven't honestly faced.

Word Study
Sin · Flesh · Death
What the original words reveal about sin's nature
ἁμαρτία
hamartia
noun, feminine — Greek
Sin; missing the mark. The general NT word for sin. From a root meaning to miss the target — originally an archery term. The mark is God's character and glory.
The most common NT word for sin. Not primarily about rule-breaking — about falling short of what God designed us to be. This makes sin both universal (everyone misses) and personal (each person's miss is specific).
παράπτωμα
paraptōma
noun, neuter — Greek
Trespass, transgression, a false step. From para (beside) + piptō (to fall). The image is of stepping off the right path — deviation from the way.
Used in Ephesians 2:1 — "dead in your trespasses and sins." Not just bad choices, but a fundamental wrong direction. The person who is trespassing isn't just lost — they're in territory they were never meant to be in.
חָטָא
chata
verb — Hebrew
To sin, to miss, to go wrong. The primary OT word for sin — same concept as hamartia: missing the mark, failing to hit the target. Also used to describe Saul's army missing stones with slings (Judges 20:16).
The word's original use (missing a target with a sling) is instructive: sin is not always deliberate rebellion. Sometimes it's simply missing what we were aimed at. Both kinds count. Both matter to God.
