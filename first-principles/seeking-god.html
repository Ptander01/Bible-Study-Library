First Principles · Study 1
Seeking God
The starting point — before anything else can be built
Guide Note — Purpose of This Study

This study surfaces whether someone is genuinely open to investigating who God is, or operating on assumptions they've never examined. Skip it for someone clearly already seeking. Use it for someone culturally Christian, religiously passive, or casually curious.

Tone: warm, unhurried, genuinely curious about them. You are not selling something. You are asking a real question together.

Opener: "Before we look at any of this — where are you starting from? Do you believe in God, are you not sure, or have you kind of assumed you do without thinking much about it?" Let them answer. Their answer tells you which section to lean into.

Where Does This Begin?

Before any study of what the Bible says — before any conversation about Jesus or the church — there is a prior question. It's the question every other question traces back to:

The First Question

Is there a God — and if there is, am I looking for him?

Everything in these studies assumes this question is being taken seriously. Not that it's been answered — but that it's open. A person who has genuinely decided the answer is no, and is not willing to reconsider, won't get much from any of this. A person who doesn't know but wants to — that's exactly where this is designed to start.

Guide Note — Watch For

The person who says "yes of course I believe in God" reflexively but has no active relationship. That's often cultural Christianity, not genuine seeking. Don't challenge it directly — let the study surface it.

The person who uses intellectual objections as a shield. Many objections to God aren't really intellectual — they're moral. "I can't believe in a God who would..." often means "I don't want to be accountable to a God who..." That's honest to name, but gently and much later in the relationship.

Is There a God?

Scripture doesn't spend much time arguing for God's existence — it assumes it. But it does point to evidence God has built into creation itself.

"For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. For his invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made."
Romans 1:19–20

Paul's argument: creation makes God knowable — not fully, not personally, but enough. The universe's existence, its fine-tuned constants for life, the existence of consciousness, of beauty, of moral intuition — none of these are accidents that explain themselves.

"The heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork."
Psalm 19:1
Guide Note — Handling Skepticism Here

If someone pushes back with science or evolution: "That's worth exploring. But let me ask — what would it take for you to believe? If no possible evidence could change your mind, that's not skepticism — that's a closed case. Real intellectual honesty has to be open in both directions."

The goal isn't to win an argument. It's to help them see the question is genuinely open — and worth pursuing honestly.

What Kind of God?

The God of Scripture is not a distant force or impersonal origin. He is a God who acts in history, who speaks — and who wants to be found.

"And he made from one man every nation of mankind to live on all the face of the earth, having determined allotted periods and the boundaries of their dwelling place, that they should seek God, and perhaps feel their way toward him and find him. Yet he is actually not far from each one of us."
Acts 17:26–27

Paul speaks to a room of sophisticated Athens skeptics and makes a bold claim: God arranged the entire arc of human history so that people would seek him. And he is not far.

Guide Note — The God Who Wants to Be Found

Many people carry an image of God as distant, demanding, or hard to reach — someone you perform for before he'll engage. Acts 17 directly contradicts that. Ask: "When you picture God, what does he look like to you — close or far? Interested or indifferent?" Let them say it. Then read Acts 17:27 slowly: "He is actually not far from each one of us."

What Does It Mean to Seek?

"Seek" in Scripture is never passive. It's not vague spirituality or general openness to religion. It carries the quality of active pursuit — someone who genuinely wants to find and adjusts their life accordingly.

"Ask, and it will be given to you; seek, and you will find; knock, and it will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives, and the one who seeks finds, and to the one who knocks it will be opened."
Matthew 7:7–8

Jesus uses three escalating verbs. Each is more active than the last. This is not waiting to see if God happens to show up. This is someone who goes looking.

Ask
Acknowledging you don't have the answer — directing your need upward. Humility before God.
Seek
Active investigation — reading, asking questions, showing up. Not waiting to see what happens.
Knock
Persistent engagement — not giving up when the first attempt doesn't produce immediate results.
Guide Note — Testing the Posture

Ask: "On a scale of 1–10, how actively are you actually looking? Or has it been more passive — open if God shows up, but not really pursuing it?" Most people land in the middle. That's honest. Help them see that seeking is something you do, not something that happens to you.

Be careful not to shame. This question is diagnostic, not accusatory.

The Condition: With All Your Heart

The promise of Jeremiah 29:13 has a condition attached. Not "you will seek me and find me" — full stop. But "when you seek me with all your heart."

"You will seek me and find me, when you seek me with all your heart."
Jeremiah 29:13
The Condition

Seek with all your heart — not a corner of it, not when convenient, not alongside everything else competing for the same space. Whole-heart seeking.

The Promise

You will find. This is not a maybe. God does not hide from the person who genuinely looks. The promise is certain — the condition is the variable.

"And without faith it is impossible to please him, for whoever would draw near to God must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who seek him."
Hebrews 11:6

Two things required to draw near to God: believing he exists, and believing he rewards those who seek him. Not certainty — but enough belief to act on. God rewards that kind of seeking.

Guide Note — The Divided Heart

The "whole heart" phrase is worth time. Most people are seeking God with part of their heart — the part not occupied by comfort, reputation, relationships, money, or habits they don't want examined. That's not seeking with all your heart.

Plant the seed: "Is there anything you're holding back from this investigation? Anything you're hoping you won't have to deal with if this turns out to be true?" Let them sit with it. Some will name it immediately. Others won't until several sessions in. Either is fine — don't excavate it aggressively.

Where Are You Starting From?

People come to this conversation from very different places. Select the one that most honestly describes where you are right now.

For the Honest Skeptic

Doubt is not the enemy of faith — incuriosity is. Job accused God of injustice. David asked why God was sleeping. Thomas refused to believe without evidence. Asaph nearly walked away entirely (Psalm 73). Real doubt, honestly brought, is the beginning of real discovery — not an obstacle to it.

"Immediately the father of the child cried out and said, 'I believe; help my unbelief!'"
Mark 9:24

This man did not resolve his doubts before coming to Jesus. He brought them with him. Jesus did not turn him away. The honest prayer — "I'm not sure, but help me" — is a prayer God has answered a thousand times.

For the Cultural Christian

Growing up in the church, in a Christian family, in the Bible Belt — none of these make someone a Christian. What you inherited is a vocabulary, a set of habits, and probably genuine affection for the idea of God. What may be missing is a personal reckoning: What do I actually believe? Have I ever genuinely sought this for myself?

"Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven."
Matthew 7:21

Jesus describes people who were religious, who used the right language, who expected to be recognized. The question he presses: not "Did you go to church?" but "Did you do the will of my Father?" That's worth sitting with honestly.

For the Believer Who Has Drifted

You believe. You've believed for years. Somewhere the distance grew — through busyness, disappointment, sin, or simply neglect — and now the relationship that once felt alive feels more like a distant memory. The good news is that God has not moved.

"Return to me, and I will return to you, says the Lord of hosts."
Malachi 3:7

The return is always available. But it requires more than nostalgia for an easier time. It requires the same whole-heart seeking Jeremiah 29 describes — active, deliberate, and honest about how far the drift has gone.

For the One Who Is Ready

Not because you have everything figured out — but because you're willing to follow wherever the investigation leads, even if it costs you something. That kind of openness is rare and it's exactly what Scripture calls "seeking with all your heart."

"But seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you."
Matthew 6:33

The promise is not that seeking God solves all your problems. It's that when you put first things first, everything else lands in its proper place. The cost of seeking is real. So is the reward.

What Gets in the Way

Comfort and Competing Loves

What most people protect when they avoid genuine seeking isn't their intellect — it's their lifestyle. If this is true, something has to change. That's the real obstacle for most people.

Inherited Religion Without Personal Reckoning

Assuming you've settled the question because your parents settled it for you. This can look like faith but has no roots — and won't survive pressure.

Intellectual Deflection

Using unresolved theological questions as a reason not to engage. Every worldview has hard questions. The question is whether you're genuinely investigating or using the questions as cover.

Passivity

Waiting for God to show up dramatically before committing to seek him. But Matthew 7 says seeking comes before finding — not after.

Guide Note — Finding the Real Obstacle

By now you should have a sense of which of these applies. The real obstacle is rarely the one named first. Intellectual objections often cover something more personal: a relationship they'd have to address, a habit they'd have to give up, a pride they'd have to lay down.

Ask: "Of these four, which most honestly describes what gets in the way for you?" Then listen without filling the silence.

The Promise That Holds

"For I know the plans I have for you, declares the Lord, plans for welfare and not for evil, to give you a future and a hope. Then you will call upon me and come and pray to me, and I will hear you. You will seek me and find me, when you seek me with all your heart."
Jeremiah 29:11–13

Jeremiah wrote this to people in exile — people with every reason to believe God had abandoned them. Into that situation, God makes a promise: I have not forgotten you. I have plans for you. When you seek me, you will find me.

That promise has not expired.

Guide Note — Closing the Conversation

End with one direct, simple question: "Are you willing to seek? Not to commit to anything yet. Not to agree with everything we've said. Just to genuinely look?" If yes, move to the challenge and agree on next steps. If unsure, affirm the honesty: "That's fair. What would need to be different for you to be willing?"

If someone is clearly not seeking and unwilling to start, don't force it. Leave the door open. The series is for people who choose to engage — not people being dragged through it.

Word Study
Seek · Heart
The original words behind the key terms of this study

Two words carry the weight of everything in this study: the word for seek and the word for heart. In both Hebrew and Greek, these words are richer and more demanding than their English translations suggest.

On Seeking

דָּרַשׁ
darash
verb — Hebrew
To seek, to inquire, to investigate with care. From a root meaning to tread or to frequent — implying repeated, persistent return rather than a single glance.
Used in Jeremiah 29:13 and Deuteronomy 4:29. The word pictures someone who returns again and again to a place — not someone who checks once and moves on. This is the seeking God requires.
בָּקַשׁ
baqash
verb — Hebrew
To seek, to search for, to desire earnestly. Implies an urgent, intentional search — like someone looking for something they need and cannot do without.
Used throughout the Psalms for seeking God's face. The word carries the weight of longing — not casual interest, but genuine need. "As the deer pants for water" (Psalm 42) is baqash territory.
ζητέω
zēteō
verb — Greek
To seek, to look for, to strive to find. Active pursuit with effort and intention. Used by Jesus in Matthew 7:7 ("seek and you will find") and Matthew 6:33 ("seek first the kingdom").
The same word describes a woman searching her house for a lost coin (Luke 15:8) — overturning everything until it's found. That quality of searching is what Jesus commends.
ἐκζητέω
ekzēteō
verb — Greek, intensive form
To seek out diligently, to search for earnestly. The prefix ek- intensifies the base — seeking that goes to the full extent, leaving nothing unsearched.
Used in Hebrews 11:6 — "he rewards those who seek him." The reward is not for casual interest. It belongs to the one whose seeking is thorough and sustained.

On the Heart

לֵבָב
levav
noun, masculine — Hebrew
The heart — the seat of the entire inner life: intellect, will, emotion, conscience. Not merely feeling, but the deepest center of who a person is.
When Scripture says "with all your heart," it means with your whole inner person — not just emotions. What you think, what you choose, and what you feel are all included. "All" leaves nothing out.
καρδία
kardia
noun, feminine — Greek
The heart — center of the spiritual and moral life. The place from which thought, desire, purpose, and decision flow. What a person truly is at their core.
Jesus says "out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery..." (Matt 15:19). The heart is the real person — not the public self, not the performed self. God seeks access to this, not the performance.
What This Means Together

Seeking God "with all your heart" — levav or kardia — is not primarily an emotional experience. It is a whole-person orientation. You bring your intellect: you actually investigate. You bring your will: you choose to pursue even when it costs you. You bring your affection: you begin to desire finding him, not just the idea of him. When all three engage together — that's seeking with all your heart.
