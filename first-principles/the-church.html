The Church
Not a building, not a service — a body
Guide Note — Purpose

Most people in a Southern context have a church background — which means they have assumptions about what church is. This study addresses those assumptions directly. Church is not a place you attend. It is a body you belong to. The distinction between spectator and participant is central to everything your congregation stands for.

Be ready for pushback about denominationalism, bad church experiences, or "I can worship God on my own." Each of these has a biblical response — but more importantly, each reveals a deeper question about what the person is actually holding back from.

What the First Church Looked Like

"And they devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers. And awe came upon every soul, and many wonders and signs were being done through the apostles. And all who believed were together and had all things in common."
Acts 2:42–44

Four marks of the first church: teaching, fellowship (koinōnia — deep sharing), breaking of bread (the Lord's Supper), and prayer. Not occasionally — they devoted themselves. And the result was awe, signs, generosity, and explosive growth.

No Spectators

There is no such thing as a solo Christian in the New Testament. Baptism is into a body, not into a private arrangement with God.

Guide Note — The Body Metaphor

1 Corinthians 12 is worth reading together. Paul's argument: a hand cannot function apart from the body. An eye cannot say to the foot "I don't need you." The same is true of Christians — each one is a member of a body, and a member detached from the body is not functioning, it's dying.

The question to ask: "What have you been giving to other Christians? Not attending — giving. What has your presence made possible for others?" If the answer is nothing, that's information.

What Church Is Not

A building

The NT church met in homes. The building is a tool. The church is the people.

A weekly service you attend

Attendance is not membership. The NT church was a daily, integrated community — not a Sunday gathering of strangers.

Optional for Christians

"Not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another" (Hebrews 10:25). Isolation is specifically warned against.

A collection of denominations you choose between

Jesus prayed for unity (John 17:20–23). Division is a failure, not a feature. The NT describes one body, one Spirit, one Lord, one faith, one baptism (Ephesians 4:4–6).

What Church Requires of You

"And let us consider how to stir up one another to love and good works, not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another, and all the more as you see the Day drawing near."
Hebrews 10:24–25

Church membership in the NT sense means you show up for others — not just for yourself. You stir up, encourage, and build up. You are a contributor, not a consumer.

Word Study
Church · Fellowship
What the original words reveal about what the church actually is
ἐκκλησία
ekklēsia
noun, feminine — Greek
Assembly, congregation, the called-out ones. From ek (out of) + kaleō (to call). In the ancient world, the ekklēsia was a called assembly — citizens summoned for a specific purpose. Not a building. A gathering of people with a shared identity and mission.
The word was used for civic assemblies before the church adopted it. It implies people who have been called out of one thing (the world, darkness) and into another (the kingdom, the body of Christ). Membership is not about preference — it's about a calling you responded to.
κοινωνία
koinōnia
noun, feminine — Greek
Fellowship, sharing, partnership, participation. From koinos — common. Deep, practical sharing of life — not just friendly conversation after a service. The early church had "all things in common" (Acts 2:44) — this is koinōnia in action.
Most English translations render this "fellowship" — which has been reduced to coffee and donuts. The real word describes people who share their money, their homes, their time, and their lives. Acts 2:44 is the definition, not the description of an ideal.
σῶμα
sōma
noun, neuter — Greek
Body — both physical body and the metaphorical body of Christ. Paul uses this image more than any other for the church: interconnected, interdependent, each part necessary for the functioning of the whole.
"Now you are the body of Christ and individually members of it" (1 Corinthians 12:27). The metaphor is deliberately physical — a hand separated from the body is not "an independent hand." It's a dead hand. Christians disconnected from the church are not "independent Christians." They're withering.
