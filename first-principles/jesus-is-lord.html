First Principles · Study 9
Jesus Is Lord
What lordship actually means — and what it demands
Guide Note — Purpose

This study addresses one of the most common failures in American Christianity: calling Jesus "Savior" but not "Lord." Many people want rescue from sin's consequences without surrender to Christ's authority. This study makes the claim direct: Jesus is Lord of everything, or he is Lord of nothing.

The Roman context of "Caesar is Lord" is worth understanding and explaining. The early Christians' confession "Jesus is Lord" was not a religious preference — it was a political act of treason. Caesar claimed the title. Christians gave it to someone else. It cost many of them their lives.

What "Lord" Actually Means

In the Roman world, "Lord" — Kyrios — was not a polite religious title. It was a claim of ultimate authority. Caesar demanded it. Slaves called their masters by it. When the first Christians said "Jesus is Lord," they were not saying "Jesus is my personal savior who helps me when I need it." They were saying: Jesus has ultimate authority over my life, over Caesar, over everything.

"Therefore God has highly exalted him and bestowed on him the name that is above every name, so that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father."
Philippians 2:9–11
The Question

Every knee will bow eventually. The only question is whether you bow now — by choice — or then, by necessity.

Guide Note — Lord vs. Savior

Many people want Jesus as Savior (rescue from hell, forgiveness of sin) without Jesus as Lord (authority over their actual life). This is not the Christianity of the NT. There is no such thing in Scripture as "Jesus is my savior but not quite my lord yet." Ask: "What area of your life, if Jesus asked you to change it tomorrow, would you most resist?"

That area is where lordship is not yet real. Name it together. This is not condemnation — it's clarity.

Lordship Is Practical

"Why do you call me 'Lord, Lord,' and not do what I tell you?"
Luke 6:46

Lordship is not a title. It is a relationship of obedience. You cannot call someone Lord and then override their instructions. That's not lordship — it's religious language applied to a relationship where you're still in charge.

1
Every area — not selected areas

Money, relationships, ambitions, sexuality, time. A lord doesn't govern some rooms in the house. He owns the house.

2
Obedience before understanding

A disciple obeys and then comes to understand — not the other way around. "If anyone's will is to do God's will, he will know" (John 7:17).

3
Ongoing, not one-time

"Take up your cross daily" — lordship is renewed every morning, not established once and left alone.

Word Study
Lord · Authority
What the original words reveal about what lordship requires
κύριος
kyrios
noun, masculine — Greek
Lord, master, owner. The Greek word used by the Septuagint (the Greek OT) to translate YHWH — the personal name of God. In the Roman Empire, it was Caesar's title. The first Christians gave it to Jesus — a direct claim that Jesus, not Caesar, is ultimate authority.
"Jesus is Lord" (Romans 10:9) was the earliest Christian creed and a dangerous one. To say kyrios Iēsous was to refuse to say kyrios Kaisar. The confession cost many believers their lives. It was not a casual religious statement — it was a comprehensive political and theological claim.
ἐξουσία
exousia
noun, feminine — Greek
Authority, power, right. The legitimate right to act, command, and be obeyed. Not power by force — but authority by right. "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" (Matthew 28:18) — Jesus' claim before the Great Commission.
Jesus' authority is not arbitrary power — it is rightful rule. The universe is his by creation and by resurrection. A Christian who acknowledges this authority but lives independently of it is living in cognitive dissonance: saying one thing, doing another.
ὑπακοή
hypakoē
noun, feminine — Greek
Obedience, submission. From hypo (under) + akouō (to hear). Literally: to hear and place yourself under what you've heard. Not reluctant compliance — attentive, responsive submission.
Romans 1:5 — "obedience of faith." Faith and obedience are not two different things — genuine faith produces obedience. You have not truly believed something if it doesn't change how you live. "Hearing under" — the very word for obedience implies that you have actually listened.
