Light & Darkness
The decision — and what it looks like to cross from one to the other
Guide Note — Purpose

This is the decision study. Everything before it has been building toward this moment: you've established what Scripture is, who God is, what you are (a sinner), what repentance means, and what the cross accomplished. Now the question is: what will you do?

Don't rush to baptism as a transaction. The person needs to understand that they're not completing a religious process — they're surrendering their life. Baptism is the outward act that corresponds to an inward reality: dying to self, being buried with Christ, and rising to a new life.

Some people are ready in this session. Others need more time. Read the room — and resist the pressure to manufacture a decision before its time. A genuine yes is worth waiting for.

There Are Only Two Kingdoms

Scripture divides humanity not into good people and bad people, religious people and secular people, or people who try and people who don't. It divides them into two kingdoms: light and darkness.

"And this is the judgment: the light has come into the world, and people loved the darkness rather than the light because their works were evil. For everyone who does wicked things hates the light and does not come to the light, lest his works should be exposed."
John 3:19–20
"He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins."
Colossians 1:13–14

Salvation is described as a transfer — from one kingdom to another. Not an improvement of the old life. A death and a resurrection into a new one.

Guide Note — The Either/Or

The language of two kingdoms is intentional and uncomfortable. There is no neutral ground in Scripture — no "spiritual but not religious," no "still figuring it out" as a permanent state. The message of this study is: you are currently in one kingdom. Jesus is calling you to cross into another. That crossing has a name — and it has a moment.

What the Crossing Looks Like

"And Peter said to them, 'Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit.'"
Acts 2:38
Repentance
The internal 180° — naming sin, turning away from it, turning toward God. Already covered in Study 5.
Faith
Trusting in Jesus — not just believing facts about him, but committing yourself to him as Lord and Savior.
Baptism
The outward act — dying with Christ, buried with him, raised with him to new life. Not a ceremony. A death.
The Spirit
God's gift to every person who responds — the Holy Spirit, who seals, transforms, and empowers the new life.

What Baptism Is

"Do you not know that all of us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death? We were buried therefore with him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, we too might walk in newness of life."
Romans 6:3–4

Baptism is not a graduation ceremony. It is a burial. The person going under the water is dying — to sin, to self-sovereignty, to the old life. The person coming up is new. The same body, the same history, but a different kingdom.

Guide Note — On Previous Baptisms

Many people in your context were baptized as infants or children without understanding what they were doing. Gently but clearly: a baptism that didn't correspond to repentance and faith is not what Acts 2:38 describes. This doesn't dishonor their family — but it does require honesty about what they're being called to now.

The question to ask: "When you were baptized, did you understand what you were doing? Were you turning away from specific sins and surrendering your life to Jesus?" If the answer is no — or "I was too young to understand" — then the previous baptism did not accomplish what Scripture describes.

Word Study
Baptism · New Birth
What the original words reveal about the crossing from darkness to light
βαπτίζω
baptizō
verb — Greek
To immerse, to dip, to plunge into. In non-biblical Greek it described dyeing cloth (the cloth was plunged into dye and came out a different color) and the sinking of ships. Not sprinkling — immersion.
The physical act matches the theological reality: going under (death) and coming up (resurrection). The cloth that comes out of the dye is permanently changed. The ship that goes under does not come back up the same. Baptism is not a gentle ceremony — it is a plunge into death with Jesus.
ἀναγεννάω
anagennaō
verb — Greek
To be born again, to be regenerated. From ana (again, above) + gennaō (to give birth). Used by Jesus in John 3:3 — unless one is born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God.
Jesus is not describing a religious experience or a renewed commitment. He is describing a birth — something that happens to you, not something you do. The new birth is from above (John 3:7 — anōthen can mean "again" or "from above"). Both are true.
σῴζω
sōzō
verb — Greek
To save, to rescue, to deliver, to heal. Used for physical rescue (from drowning, from illness) and spiritual salvation. Not a transaction — an ongoing rescue by someone who has the power to save.
"Whoever calls on the name of the Lord will be saved" (Acts 2:21). The word encompasses the whole of what Jesus does — forgives sin, heals the broken, restores the lost, defeats death. Salvation is not a destination. It is being rescued by a person.
