The Word of God
Why we trust this book — and what we do with it
Guide Note — Purpose

Before someone can be shaped by Scripture, they need to settle whether it's trustworthy. This study isn't a defense of inerrancy — it's an invitation to treat the Bible as what it claims to be: God's own word. The goal is confidence, not just intellectual assent.

Many people have a low-grade suspicion that the Bible is just a human book full of good advice. This study confronts that directly. Don't rush past the God-breathed concept — it's the hinge everything else turns on.

What Kind of Book Is This?

Most people treat the Bible like an ancient self-help book — inspiring, occasionally useful, but ultimately a human product shaped by its time. That's not what it claims to be.

The Central Claim

This book did not originate with men. It originated with God — and it is useful precisely because of that.

"All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness."
2 Timothy 3:16
Guide Note — On Theo-pneustos

Spend time on the word "breathed out." God spoke the universe into existence. The same creative breath is behind Scripture. It's not that God approved what humans wrote — it's that God produced it through them. That's a different claim entirely.

Ask: "If this book really is God speaking — not just about God, but God actually speaking — how would that change how you read it?"

How Did God Produce It?

"For no prophecy was ever produced by the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit."
2 Peter 1:21

God worked through the authors' own personalities, experiences, and writing styles. The origin is divine. The delivery is human. Both are true at the same time.

Teaching
Establishes what is true — the foundation for faith and life.
Reproof
Confronts what is wrong — sin, error, dangerous patterns of thinking.
Correcting
Shows how to get back on track — restoration and realignment.
Training
Equips for ongoing growth — developing godly character over time.

How Jesus Treated Scripture

The most compelling evidence for the Bible's authority is how Jesus himself handled it.

1
He quoted it in temptation

"It is written..." — three times in Matthew 4. Jesus did not reason his way through Satan's attacks. He quoted Scripture as settled authority.

2
He declared it unbreakable

"Scripture cannot be broken" (John 10:35). Not "Scripture is generally reliable." Not "most of Scripture." Cannot be broken.

3
He opened it to the disciples

Luke 24:27 — beginning with Moses and all the prophets, he interpreted to them what concerned himself in all the Scriptures.

Guide Note — The Practical Question

Circle back to usefulness: "Paul says it's profitable. What would it look like to actually use it — in your marriage, your decisions, how you handle conflict? Or are you currently going to other sources first?" This connects the theological claim to the daily reality.

Word Study
Breath · Prophecy
The original words behind Scripture's authority claims
θεόπνευστος
theopneustos
adjective — Greek
God-breathed; divinely inspired. Compound of theos (God) + pneō (to breathe or blow). The same root as pneuma — Spirit, breath, wind.
Used only once in the NT — 2 Timothy 3:16. The word choice is remarkable: not "approved by God" or "about God" — but breathed out from God himself. The same creative breath that spoke the cosmos into existence.
γραφή
graphē
noun, feminine — Greek
Writing, Scripture. Used 51 times in the NT — always referring to the sacred writings of the Old Testament, treated by Jesus and the apostles as authoritative and binding.
"Scripture cannot be broken" (John 10:35) uses this word. The graphē is not a collection of wise thoughts — it is a binding, unbreakable word. Jesus treated it that way.
דָּבָר
dabar
noun, masculine — Hebrew
Word, thing, matter, event. God's dabar is not merely speech — it is effective action. When God speaks, things happen. His word does not return empty (Isaiah 55:11).
The same word used for "In the beginning God said..." (Genesis 1). The word of God creates reality. Scripture is not advice — it is the creative, governing word of the Creator.
