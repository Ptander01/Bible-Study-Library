Counting the Cost
The final question — not can you, but will you
Guide Note — Purpose

This is the final study of the series — the summation and the invitation. Everything has been laid out: what Scripture is, who God is, who you are, what repentance means, what the cross accomplished, what it means to say Jesus is Lord, and what the church requires. Now the question is simple and total: Will you follow?

Jesus himself urges people to count the cost before committing. This study takes that seriously. Don't rush to a decision. Don't manufacture one. But do make the question clear and direct: this is what it costs. Are you in?

This study is sometimes the end of the series for someone who isn't ready. That's okay. Leave the door open. A "not yet" that's honest is far better than a "yes" that falls apart in six months.

Jesus Urges You to Count First

"For which of you, desiring to build a tower, does not first sit down and count the cost, whether he has enough to complete it? Otherwise, when he has laid a foundation and is not able to finish, all who see it begin to mock him, saying, 'This man began to build and was not able to finish.'"
Luke 14:28–30

This is remarkable: Jesus is actively discouraging impulsive commitment. He doesn't want people following him halfway and then stopping. A half-built tower is worse than no tower — it becomes a monument to failure. Jesus wants counted, deliberate, fully-informed commitment.

What This Series Has Shown

The Bible is God's word — trustworthy and authoritative. You are a sinner in need of rescue. Jesus died for your sin and rose from the dead. Repentance means turning completely. Baptism is dying with Christ and rising to new life. The church is a body you belong to. Jesus is Lord of everything. This is what you're saying yes to.

What It Will Cost

"So therefore, any one of you who does not renounce all that he has cannot be my disciple."
Luke 14:33
Your self-sovereignty

You are no longer the final authority in your own life. Jesus is Lord — which means his word governs your decisions, not just informs them.

Some relationships

"Do not be deceived: Bad company ruins good morals" (1 Corinthians 15:33). Some relationships will be strained. Some may end. Others will be transformed.

Your comfort

"In the world you will have tribulation" (John 16:33). Following Jesus is not a promise of ease. It is a promise of his presence in difficulty.

Your reputation

"If they persecuted me, they will also persecute you" (John 15:20). In some contexts, identifying with Jesus has real social cost.

Guide Note — The Real Cost

After going through the costs, ask: "Which one of these is the actual sticking point for you?" Let them name it. Then sit with it — don't immediately offer a workaround or a reassurance. The cost is real. Jesus said so. Let the weight of it land.

Then: "Now consider what you're holding onto instead. What is your life without Jesus — actually, not theoretically?" The cost of not following is also worth counting.

What It Is Worth

"The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and covered up. Then in his joy he goes and sells all that he has and buys that field."
Matthew 13:44

The man doesn't sell everything reluctantly. He sells everything in his joy — because he has found something worth more than everything he's giving up. The cost is real. So is what it buys.

"I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us."
Romans 8:18

The Final Question

The Only Question Left

You've counted the cost. You know what it requires. You know what it's worth. Will you follow?

Guide Note — The Response

If yes: don't delay. Walk through what happens next — repentance, baptism, and receiving the Holy Spirit. If they haven't been baptized as a believer who understands what they're doing, that's the next step. Today if possible.

If not yet: honor the honesty. Ask what specific thing is still unresolved. Keep the relationship. Leave the door open. Some people need another pass through one of the earlier studies. Some need time. Don't manufacture a yes — but don't let the conversation end without clarity about what they're choosing and what they're walking away from.

If no: be honest about what that means. They are choosing to remain in darkness. Name that without condemnation — but name it clearly. You are not doing them a favor by softening it. Jesus didn't soften it. "No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back is fit for the kingdom of God" (Luke 9:62).

Word Study
Cost · Treasure
The original words behind counting the cost and finding the treasure
ψηφίζω
psēphizō
verb — Greek
To count, to calculate, to reckon. From psēphos — a pebble used as a counting stone in ancient accounting. The precise, deliberate act of tallying — making sure you have enough before you begin.
Luke 14:28 — "sit down and count the cost." The sitting down matters. This is not an impulsive decision made in an emotional moment. Jesus wants deliberate, informed, calculated commitment — not enthusiasm that evaporates when difficulty arrives.
θησαυρός
thēsauros
noun, masculine — Greek
Treasure, storehouse, something of great value stored away. The word behind "thesaurus" — a storehouse of words. In Matthew 13:44, the treasure hidden in a field — what the kingdom of heaven is worth.
The man who finds the treasure doesn't agonize over selling everything. He sells "in his joy." The cost only looks like loss from the outside. From inside — when you've found the treasure — the math is obvious. Everything you give up is less valuable than what you've found.
τελέω
teleō
verb — Greek
To complete, to finish, to bring to an end. From telos — the end, the goal, the purpose. Jesus uses this word in the tower-building parable: the danger is starting and not being able to finish. The same root as tetelestai — "It is finished" — on the cross.
The half-built tower in Luke 14 is a monument to failed commitment. Jesus uses the same root word for "finishing" that he uses at the cross when the work of salvation is completed. He finished his work. The question is whether you will finish yours.
