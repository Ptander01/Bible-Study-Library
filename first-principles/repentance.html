Repentance
Not remorse — a complete change of direction
Guide Note — Purpose

This study directly follows sin. The person should now have specific sins named. The question is: what do you do with them? Repentance is not the same as feeling bad. Many people have felt bad about their sin for years without repenting. The difference is direction.

The distinction between worldly sorrow and godly sorrow (2 Corinthians 7:10) is the heart of this study. Make sure it lands — it explains why so many people cycle through guilt without ever actually changing.

Feeling Bad Is Not Repentance

Most people, when confronted with sin, feel some level of guilt or regret. That feeling is not repentance. It is possible to feel terrible about what you've done and never actually change direction.

"For godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death."
2 Corinthians 7:10
Worldly grief
Sorrow about consequences — embarrassment, damaged reputation, getting caught. Focused on self. Produces death.
Godly grief
Sorrow about the sin itself — grief over breaking relationship with God, over the wrong done. Focused on God. Produces repentance.
Guide Note — The Test

Ask: "When you've felt guilty about sin in the past, what were you most sorry about — the thing itself, or what it cost you?" Most people have only experienced worldly sorrow. Help them see the difference — and why it matters.

Judas felt terrible about betraying Jesus. He gave the money back. He confessed publicly. He died by suicide. That is worldly grief taken to its extreme — producing death. Peter also denied Jesus. He also wept bitterly. But Peter's sorrow was oriented toward Jesus — and it produced restoration, not destruction.

What Repentance Actually Means

"Repent therefore, and turn back, that your sins may be blotted out."
Acts 3:19

The Greek word metanoia — repentance — means a change of mind so complete that it changes direction. It is not a mood. It is not a feeling. It is a 180° reorientation of the entire person — mind, will, and action — away from sin and toward God.

1
See it clearly

Name the specific sin — not "I've made some mistakes" but "I did this specific thing and it was wrong before God."

2
Feel it rightly

Godly grief — sorrow oriented toward God, not toward consequences. "I have sinned against heaven and before you" (Luke 15:21).

3
Turn completely

Stop moving in that direction. This is not willpower alone — it is a decision followed by a new pattern of life.

4
Bear fruit

"Bear fruit in keeping with repentance" (Matthew 3:8). Real repentance produces visible change — not just a declaration.

What God Does With Repented Sin

"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."
1 John 1:9

The promise of forgiveness is certain — but it is conditional on honest confession, not just a general sense of being sorry. "All unrighteousness" — complete, not partial cleansing.

Guide Note — Closing

This is the first study where someone may want to pray, confess, and actually repent in the conversation. Be ready for that. Don't manufacture it — but don't avoid it either. If they ask "so what do I do right now?" walk them through honest, specific confession to God. Then connect it to the next study: the cross is where this repentance finds its ground.

Take the list of sins you named last week. For each one: confess it specifically to God. Not "forgive my sins" — but "I sinned against you when I ___." Then ask: what does turning away from this actually look like in my daily life? Write it down.

Word Study
Repentance · Turn
What the original words reveal about what repentance really requires
μετάνοια
metanoia
noun, feminine — Greek
Repentance; a complete change of mind and direction. From meta (after, change) + nous (mind). Not remorse alone — a 180° reorientation of the whole person.
Worldly sorrow feels bad about sin. Metanoia changes direction entirely. Acts 2:38 requires this — not just an emotional response. John the Baptist demanded it as a precondition (Matthew 3:2). Jesus' first recorded words in Mark are "repent" (Mark 1:15).
ἐπιστρέφω
epistrephō
verb — Greek
To turn around, to return, to convert. The physical image of someone walking in one direction who stops, turns, and walks the other way. Often paired with metanoia in the NT.
Acts 3:19 uses both words: "repent and turn back." Metanoia is the internal change of mind. Epistrephō is the external change of direction. Real repentance includes both — the inner turn and the outward new trajectory.
שׁוּב
shuv
verb — Hebrew
To turn, to return, to go back. The primary OT word for repentance. Deeply relational — it describes returning to a person (God), not just stopping a behavior.
Used over 1000 times in the OT. "Return to me, and I will return to you" (Malachi 3:7) uses shuv. Repentance is not primarily about stopping bad behavior — it's about returning to God. The behavior change follows from the relational turn.
