Disciplemaking
The fourth pillar — and the one that proves the other three are real

The Logic of the Four

The Core Four build on each other. A person saturated in the gospel understands what they've received and who they serve. A person committed to unconditional discipleship is being formed into the image of Christ. A person devoted to community has a life intertwined with others. All three of those eventually produce the fourth: someone who cannot help but bring others along.

Disciplemaking is not a program or a role — it is the natural outcome of the first three pillars working in someone's life. People who are genuinely living in the gospel, genuinely following Jesus, genuinely in community with others — those people are making disciples, whether or not they call it that.

"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful people who will be able to teach others also."
2 Timothy 2:2

Four generations in one verse: Paul → Timothy → faithful people → others also. This is the shape of disciplemaking — not addition (you plus one), but multiplication. Each person who is genuinely discipled becomes capable of discipling others. The math changes everything.

Paul never met most of the people his ministry reached. He didn't have to — he invested deeply in Timothy, who invested in faithful people, who taught others. The kingdom grows through this pattern. Every generation of disciples who disciples others is participating in something that extends far beyond what they can see.

The Three Methods

Disciplemaking is not complicated. Jesus modeled three methods, and they work as simply now as they did in the first century. They don't require a curriculum, a formal program, or specialized training. They require intention, honesty, and time.

Model Life
The most powerful teaching you do is not what you say — it's what you are. Paul tells the Corinthians: "Be imitators of me, as I am of Christ" (1 Corinthians 11:1). That requires you to be willing to be known — to let someone close enough to actually see your life. You can't model what you hide.
Pray Together
Disciplemaking is not your work — it is God's. You are not the one doing the changing. Prayer keeps you honest about that. Praying together with the person you're discipling is both a practice of community (Pillar 3) and a declaration of dependence: we need the Spirit to do what we cannot. It also models prayer as a normal, non-performative part of life.
Teach to Obey
Matthew 28:20 — not "teach them to know" but "teach them to observe all that I have commanded." The goal of your teaching is not their comprehension — it's their obedience. Every conversation, every study, every passage should end with: so what do you do with this? Accountability is not optional; it's the follow-through of teaching.

What Disciplemaking Requires

Reflection
You can't give what you haven't received. Before you can help someone else examine their life, you have to have examined your own. Disciplemaking starts with honest self-awareness — not perfection, but genuine engagement with your own growth.
Courage
It requires courage to enter someone's life at depth. To ask hard questions. To say true things. To follow up when they don't follow through. To stay when it's awkward. Most people avoid disciplemaking not because they don't believe in it, but because it's uncomfortable.
Honesty
You must be honest about your own life — your failures, your struggles, your ongoing formation. A disciplemaker who projects perfection is not useful. The person you're discipling needs to see someone who is genuinely being changed, not someone who pretends they've arrived.
Consistency
Disciplemaking works through sustained relationship over time. A single intense conversation accomplishes less than regular, ordinary presence. Show up. Keep showing up. The long faithfulness is what forms people.

The Methods Are Simple

A Word on Complexity

There is an industry built around discipleship tools, programs, and curricula. Some of them are useful. None of them is the point. Jesus made disciples with no curriculum and no program — he walked with people, ate with people, let people watch him pray, asked hard questions, and sent them out to try what they'd learned.

The teaching methods of disciplemaking are simple. They are intended for two or three people in a living room or across a table, not for a stage. What they require is not sophistication. They require reflection, courage, and honesty — in you first, then offered to others.

01
I Do, You Watch

Jesus let his disciples watch him. He prayed in front of them, healed in front of them, confronted Pharisees in front of them. Effective disciplemaking starts here — invite someone into your actual life, not a polished presentation of it.

02
I Do, You Help

Then you bring them alongside — they participate while you lead. Peter is present at the Transfiguration. The disciples help distribute the loaves. Involvement before independence produces better formation than information before involvement.

03
You Do, I Help

Then the roles shift. Jesus sends the Twelve out in pairs (Luke 9) and the Seventy-Two (Luke 10) — before his resurrection, before Pentecost, before they fully understand what they're doing. They learn by doing with oversight. Debrief is essential: what happened? What did you learn?

04
You Do, I Watch

Eventually the disciple is doing what the disciplemaker was doing — and the disciplemaker steps back. This is the goal: not dependency, but reproduction. The fully formed disciple is one who can disciple others. 2 Timothy 2:2 — entrust to faithful people who will be able to teach others also.

Word Study · Pillar 04
Entrust · Faithful · Multiply
The original words behind the multiplication vision
παρατίθημι
paratithēmi
verb — Greek
To entrust; to deposit; to hand over for safekeeping. Used for depositing valuables with a trustworthy person, as a banker or guardian. In 2 Timothy 2:2, Paul tells Timothy to entrust what he has received to others — as if passing along something precious and irreplaceable.
The word implies the gospel is not yours to keep — it was deposited with you to be passed on. A disciple who receives the gospel and holds it without passing it forward has misunderstood the nature of what was given. What is entrusted is meant to be re-entrusted. Disciplemaking is the logic of paratithēmi.
πιστός
pistos
adjective — Greek
Faithful; trustworthy; reliable. From pisteuō — to believe, to trust. A pistos person is one who can be trusted with something — whose life and character make them a reliable vessel for what you give them. Paul doesn't say "entrust to talented people" or "gifted people" — faithful people.
This reframes what you look for in a person to disciple. The question is not: "who is most capable?" but "who is most faithful?" A faithful person with limited gifts will reproduce more faithfully than a talented person with divided loyalty. Jesus chose fishermen, not theologians. Faithfulness is the soil in which discipleship takes root.
καρποφορέω
karpophoreō
verb — Greek
To bear fruit; to be fruitful; to produce results. From karpos (fruit) + phoreō (to bear, to wear). Jesus uses the agricultural metaphor consistently — the vine and the branches, the sower and the soil, the fig tree. Fruitfulness is expected of a disciple, and the fruit is not primarily personal transformation — it is other disciples.
John 15:16 — "I chose you and appointed you that you should go and bear fruit and that your fruit should abide." The fruit that abides is not feelings or experiences — it is other people changed by the gospel, now following Jesus themselves. The disciplemaker's life is measured by this: not what they've accumulated, but what they've passed on.
Scripture · Pillar 04
Key Passages
The biblical foundation for passionate disciplemaking
The Commission
"All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."
Matthew 28:18–20The command to make disciples is the direct consequence of the King's authority. It cannot be outsourced to professionals.
"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful people who will be able to teach others also."
2 Timothy 2:2Four generations in one sentence. The vision is multiplication, not addition.
The Method
"Be imitators of me, as I am of Christ."
1 Corinthians 11:1The disciplemaker's permission — and their accountability. You must be able to say this honestly.
"Him we proclaim, warning everyone and teaching everyone with all wisdom, that we may present everyone mature in Christ. For this I toil, struggling with all his energy that he powerfully works within me."
Colossians 1:28–29Everyone. Mature in Christ. Paul labors for this — not performs it. Disciplemaking is hard work done in dependence on God's energy.
The Sending
"And he called the twelve and began to send them out two by two, and gave them authority over the unclean spirits… So they went out and proclaimed that people should repent."
Mark 6:7, 12Sent before fully formed. The sending is part of the formation — you learn by going, not just by watching.
"I chose you and appointed you that you should go and bear fruit and that your fruit should abide, so that whatever you ask the Father in my name, he may give it to you."
John 15:16Fruit that abides — not momentary results but lasting transformation in others. This is the disciplemaker's goal.
The Foundation
"So we, though many, are one body in Christ, and individually members one of another. Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us, let us use them."
Romans 12:5–6Disciplemaking happens in the body. What you have been given is for others — use it.
"Follow the pattern of the sound words that you have heard from me, in the faith and love that are in Christ Jesus. By the Holy Spirit who dwells within us, guard the good deposit entrusted to you."
2 Timothy 1:13–14Guard the deposit — and pass it on. Both. The disciplemaker is a faithful steward, not an owner.
