The Gospel
What it actually is — and why most people have only heard half of it

What Is the Gospel?

Ask most Christians what the gospel is and you'll get something like: "God loves you, Jesus died for your sins, believe and go to heaven." That's not wrong — but it's incomplete. And incomplete gospels produce incomplete disciples.

The word gospel — euangelion in Greek — means royal announcement. It's the word used in the Roman world when a general won a battle, or when a new king came to power. A herald would run ahead of the army and announce: "The king has come. The battle is won. Everything has changed." That's what the gospel is.

"The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand; repent and believe in the gospel."
Mark 1:15 — Jesus' opening proclamation

Jesus didn't open with "God loves you." He opened with a royal announcement: the Kingdom has arrived. Repent and believe the news. The gospel is the announcement that Jesus is King and his Kingdom is now available to all who will enter it.

What Christ Means

"Christ" is not Jesus' last name. It is a title — the Greek translation of the Hebrew Mashiach (Messiah), meaning Anointed One. Anointing in the Old Testament commissioned kings, priests, and prophets. To call Jesus "Christ" is to declare him King, High Priest, and Prophet all in one. When Paul opens every letter "from Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus" — he is placing himself as a servant of a King.

The Gospel Paul Preached

"Now I would remind you, brothers, of the gospel I preached to you… that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures."
1 Corinthians 15:1–4
He Was Born
The eternal Son of God entered human history — not as an observer but as a participant. He lived the life we were supposed to live, in full obedience to the Father, as the true image-bearing human.
He Died
For the sins of his people. Not as a martyr or a victim — as a sacrifice. The righteous for the unrighteous. He absorbed the full weight of the world's sin and the full wrath of a holy God.
He Rose
On the third day — declared Son of God with power (Romans 1:4). The resurrection is the Father's verdict: the sacrifice was accepted. Death is defeated. He is who he said he was.
He Reigns
Ascended and seated at the right hand of the Father. All authority in heaven and on earth belongs to him (Matthew 28:18). This is the part most people leave out — and it's what makes the gospel news rather than just a nice story.

The Gospel vs. The Benefits of the Gospel

Here's where things go wrong. Most of what gets preached as "the gospel" is actually the benefits of the gospel — what you receive when you receive the King. These are real and wonderful:

Benefits — Real but Secondary
What you receive

Adoption and purpose
Forgiveness of sins
The indwelling Holy Spirit
Entrance into the Kingdom
Freedom from bondage
Eternal life with God

The Gospel — The Announcement
What has happened

Jesus is King
The Kingdom has come
Death is defeated
The world is being restored
There is a new humanity
Everything has changed

When we lead with the benefits and omit the announcement, we produce consumers — people who come to Jesus for what he gives rather than for who he is. A gospel-saturated disciple has heard and received both: the announcement that King Jesus reigns, and the personal reality of what that means for them.

The Expectation That Follows

The gospel is not merely information to be believed. It is an announcement that demands a response. When a new king comes to power, you don't simply acknowledge him intellectually — you align yourself with his kingdom. The call of the gospel is not "agree with this" but repent and believe — turn your whole life toward the King and his Kingdom.

Matthew 4:17–19: Jesus says "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" — and immediately calls fishermen to leave their nets. Follow me. The announcement produces followers, not just believers.

What "Saturated" Means

The first of the Core Four is not merely "knows the gospel" — it's saturated in it. The difference matters. A person who knows the gospel can recite it. A person saturated in the gospel is shaped by it — it is the lens through which they see everything else.

In Identity
A gospel-saturated person knows they are defined by what God says, not what they've done or what others think. Adoption replaces performance. Security replaces striving.
In Relationships
Because they've received grace they didn't earn, they give grace they're not owed. Forgiveness flows from the gospel — "as God in Christ forgave you" (Ephesians 4:32).
In Suffering
The resurrection means the worst thing is never the last thing. A saturated disciple faces hard things with hope — not denial, but the deep confidence that God is at work and death doesn't win.
In Mission
People saturated in the gospel want others to have what they have. Acts 8:4 — "those who were scattered went about preaching the word." The gospel announced produces people who announce the gospel.

Word Study · Pillar 01
Gospel · King · Kingdom
The original words behind the royal announcement
εὐαγγέλιον
euangelion
noun, neuter — Greek
Gospel; good news; royal announcement. From eu (good) + angelos (messenger). In the Roman world, a euangelion was the official announcement of a military victory or the accession of a new emperor. Not a private letter — a public proclamation that changed the political reality for everyone who heard it.
This is why Paul says he is "not ashamed of the gospel" (Romans 1:16) — the word itself was associated with Roman imperial power. Paul is announcing a greater King and a greater kingdom. The gospel is not a self-help message; it is a royal decree.
Χριστός
Christos
noun, masculine — Greek
Anointed One; Messiah; King. The Greek translation of the Hebrew Mashiach. In the Old Testament, anointing was the ceremony that commissioned kings (1 Samuel 16:13), priests (Exodus 29:7), and prophets (1 Kings 19:16). To call Jesus "Christ" is to declare him all three.
By the time of Paul's letters, "Christ" was already being used almost as a name — many of his Gentile readers may not have known its meaning. Paul in Romans 1:1–4 takes time to unpack it: this is the gospel "concerning his Son… declared to be the Son of God in power… Jesus Christ our Lord." The title carries the full weight of Israel's royal expectation.
βασιλεία
basileia
noun, feminine — Greek
Kingdom; reign; royal rule. Not primarily a territory but an exercise of authority. The kingdom of God is wherever God's rule is acknowledged and operative. Jesus announced its arrival — not as something entirely future, but as a present reality breaking into the world through him.
Matthew 4:17 — "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand." The word translated "at hand" (ēngiken) means it has drawn near, has arrived at your doorstep. Jesus' miracles are signs of the kingdom's presence — disease fleeing, demons expelled, the dead raised. The kingdom is not just something to wait for. It is something to enter now.

Key Passages
The biblical foundation for the gospel as royal announcement
What the Gospel Is
"Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle, set apart for the gospel of God, which he promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures, concerning his Son, who was descended from David according to the flesh and was declared to be the Son of God in power according to the Spirit of holiness by his resurrection from the dead, Jesus Christ our Lord."
Romans 1:1–4Paul's opening definition — the gospel is about a person, a resurrection, a declaration of Kingship.
"The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand; repent and believe in the gospel."
Mark 1:15Jesus' first public words — a royal announcement, not a therapy offer.
"For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures."
1 Corinthians 15:3–4The core events — the "of first importance" gospel that Paul received and passed on.
The Response the Gospel Demands
"And Jesus came and said to them, 'All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you.'"
Matthew 28:18–20The commission flows from the reign. Because he is King over everything, go make subjects.
"Philip went down to the city of Samaria and proclaimed to them the Christ… But when they believed Philip as he preached good news about the kingdom of God and the name of Jesus Christ, they were baptized, both men and women."
Acts 8:5, 12Note what Philip preached: the kingdom of God AND the name of Jesus. Both together. That's the full gospel.
The Benefits That Follow
"In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace… In him you also, when you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation, and believed in him, were sealed with the promised Holy Spirit."
Ephesians 1:7, 13Forgiveness, adoption, the Spirit — real benefits, flowing from the King.
"The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor. He has sent me to proclaim liberty to the captives and recovering of sight to the blind, to set at liberty those who are oppressed."
Luke 4:18Jesus quoting Isaiah 61 — the gospel brings liberation. Freedom is a benefit, not the announcement itself.
Saturated — Living in the Gospel
"For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek."
Romans 1:16The gospel is not a private comfort — it is the power of God, publicly announced.
"Now those who were scattered because of the persecution that arose over Stephen traveled as far as Phoenicia and Cyprus and Antioch, speaking the word to no one except Jews. But there were some of them… who on coming to Antioch spoke to the Hellenists also, preaching the Lord Jesus."
Acts 11:19–20Saturated people can't help it — everywhere they go, the announcement follows them.
