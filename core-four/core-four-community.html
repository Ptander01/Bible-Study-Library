Community
Not just where we gather — who we are to each other

The Question Nobody Is Asking

Most conversations about community in the church are about the local church — should you attend, which one, how often. But the New Testament question is different. It's not "are you part of a local church?" The question is: are you actually in community?

You can attend a church for years and not be known by anyone. You can sit in the same room with people every week and have no idea what's actually happening in their lives. That's not community. That's proximity.

"And they devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers. And awe came upon every soul, and many wonders and signs were being done through the apostles. And all who believed were together and had all things in common."
Acts 2:42–44

The first church didn't organize small groups or launch a community program. They simply could not stop being together. The gospel had done something to them — bound them to each other in a way that made natural, ordinary life together inevitable.

What Devoted Means

The Word

Acts 2:42 says they devoted themselves — the Greek is proskartereō: to be persistently attentive to, to continue steadfastly in, to hold on to with strength. It's the same word used for soldiers who held their post. Not casual engagement — committed, persistent presence with each other over time.

Devotion to community is not the same as being friendly, or enjoying church, or showing up reliably. It means your life is actually intertwined with other people's lives — you know their struggles, they know yours, you carry each other's burdens, you celebrate together, you correct each other in love. That kind of community doesn't happen accidentally. It requires decision and devotion.

What Real Community Rejects

Many people who attend church are in one of these modes rather than genuine community. Honest self-examination starts here:

The One Another Commands

The New Testament contains over 50 "one another" commands — instructions about how members of the body of Christ are to treat each other. They only make sense in the context of real, close, ongoing relationship. You cannot obey them at arm's length.

Love One Another
John 13:34–35
As I have loved you. The standard is the cross. This is the witness to the world — how you treat each other.
Honor One Another
Romans 12:10
Be devoted, preferring each other in honor. In a culture of self-promotion, the body of Christ competes to honor rather than to be honored.
Bear One Another's Burdens
Galatians 6:2
You cannot carry someone's burden without being close enough to know what it is. This requires disclosure and trust from both parties.
Confess to One Another
James 5:16
And pray for each other, that you may be healed. Confession requires someone you trust. It requires a real relationship, not an acquaintance.
Admonish One Another
Romans 15:14; Colossians 3:16
Instruction, correction, warning. This is only possible — and only received — in the context of established trust and mutual care.
Encourage One Another
1 Thessalonians 5:11; Hebrews 10:25
Daily, while it is still called today (Hebrews 3:13). Not periodic inspiration — ongoing, daily presence with each other's lives.
Serve One Another
Galatians 5:13
Through love, be servants of each other. Freedom in Christ expressed as service — the opposite of using the community for your benefit.
Submit to One Another
Ephesians 5:21
Out of reverence for Christ. Mutual submission is the community's internal structure — not a hierarchy of who gets their way.
Pray for One Another
James 5:16
Not just for each other — with each other. Praying together is one of the clearest markers of genuine community.

Hospitality as Community

The New Testament consistently connects community to the practice of hospitality — opening your home, your table, your life. The early church met in homes. The shared meal was not optional; it was where community happened. Romans 12:13 commands it; 1 Peter 4:9 repeats it without grumbling.

What Hospitality Is

The Greek word is philoxenia — love of strangers. Not just having friends over, but making space for others in your life. An open home produces open relationships. People who practice hospitality have lives that are available — not perfectly curated, but genuinely accessible to others.

Consistency matters as much as warmth. An occasional open home is nice. A consistently open life is community.

This Week
Look at the one another list and ask: which of these am I actually practicing with someone? Which require a closer relationship than you currently have? Who could you invite into your home this week — not to impress, but to begin?

Word Study · Pillar 03
Fellowship · Body · Devoted
What the original words reveal about Christian community
κοινωνία
koinōnia
noun, feminine — Greek
Fellowship; sharing; participation; partnership. From koinos — common, shared. Not a feeling of warmth toward people, but an actual sharing of things held in common — life, resources, struggle, joy. The root idea is joint ownership and participation.
Acts 2:42 — they devoted themselves to the koinōnia. 1 John 1:3 — "our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ." The vertical and horizontal are connected: genuine fellowship with God produces genuine fellowship with each other. You cannot have one while refusing the other.
σῶμα
sōma
noun, neuter — Greek
Body. Paul's primary metaphor for the church — not an organization, not a gathering, but a body. A body is composed of different parts that do different things, are mutually dependent, share one life, and cannot function correctly when a part is missing or diseased.
1 Corinthians 12:26 — "If one member suffers, all suffer together; if one member is honored, all rejoice together." This is not a metaphor you can hold at arm's length. In a body, what happens to one part affects all the others. Real community means your pain is felt in the body, and others' pain is felt by you. That requires proximity.
προσκαρτερέω
proskartereō
verb — Greek
To devote oneself to; to be steadfastly attentive to; to continue steadfastly in; to hold fast to something. From pros (toward) + karterō (to be strong, to endure). The word was used of soldiers holding their post — persistent, committed presence that doesn't waver when it's inconvenient.
Acts 2:42 — "they devoted themselves." Not "they occasionally gathered" or "they attended when convenient." The first church's community was defined by persistent, faithful presence with each other. The same word appears in Acts 1:14 for the disciples praying together before Pentecost, and in Romans 12:12 for persisting in prayer. It is a word for commitment that holds.
Scripture · Pillar 03
Key Passages
The biblical foundation for devoted community
The First Church
"And they devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers. And awe came upon every soul… And all who believed were together and had all things in common. And they were selling their possessions and belongings and distributing the proceeds to all, as any had need. And day by day, attending the temple together and breaking bread in their homes, they received their food with glad and generous hearts."
Acts 2:42–46The template — devotion, fellowship, shared table, prayer, daily life together. Not a program. A life.
The Body
"For just as the body is one and has many members, and all the members of the body, though many, are one body, so it is with Christ… If one member suffers, all suffer together; if one member is honored, all rejoice together."
1 Corinthians 12:12, 26One body. What happens to one affects all. This is community — not proximity.
"Rather, speaking the truth in love, we are to grow up in every way into him who is the head, into Christ, from whom the whole body, joined and held together by every joint with which it is equipped, when each part is working properly, makes the body grow so that it builds itself up in love."
Ephesians 4:15–16Every part working. Every joint connected. The body builds itself up — but only when each part is active.
The One Anothers
"A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another."
John 13:34–35The witness to the world is not arguments won — it's love displayed between believers.
"And let us consider how to stir up one another to love and good works, not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another, and all the more as you see the Day drawing near."
Hebrews 10:24–25Stir up. Not just show up — actively provoke each other toward love and good works. This requires relationship, not just attendance.
"Therefore, confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed."
James 5:16Healing happens in community. This is not a private transaction between you and God — it involves another person.
