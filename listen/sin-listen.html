Sin
"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."
Romans 3:23
This is the study that makes every other study make sense. Without a real understanding of sin, the cross is just a historical event and repentance is just a mood shift. The goal here isn't guilt — it's clarity. An honest look in the mirror before anything else.
The word "all" in Romans 3:23 leaves no exceptions. Religious background, good intentions, community standing — none of these change the diagnosis. Every person who has ever lived, except Jesus, has sinned.
Part One
The Universal Diagnosis
"For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."
Romans 6:23
The word for sin in Greek is hamartia — to miss the mark. The mark is God's character and glory. Every time we fall short of what God designed us to be, that's sin. This is broader and more personal than a list of bad behaviors.
Jesus locates the source of sin not in environment or upbringing, but in the heart. From within, out of the heart of man, come evil thoughts, sexual immorality, theft, murder, adultery, coveting, wickedness, deceit, sensuality, envy, slander, pride, foolishness. This is why external moral improvement can't solve the problem — the problem is internal.
"But your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear."
Isaiah 59:2
Sin doesn't just produce guilt. It creates distance from God — a real, relational rupture. You cannot draw close to a holy God while holding onto the things that separate you from him.
Reflect
When you think about sin, what comes to mind? Is it mostly things other people do — or things you do? Has the problem ever felt personally real to you?
Take a moment with that before moving on.
Part Two
The Sin Lists
The New Testament contains specific lists of sins — not to pile on guilt, but to create the specificity that vague remorse can't produce. General guilt produces general repentance. Naming specific things is what makes real change possible.
As you listen, ask yourself honestly: is this me? Have I seen this in my own life?
From Galatians 5

Sexual immorality — any sexual activity outside God's design. Impurity — moral uncleanness in thought, word, or deed. Sensuality — unrestrained indulgence without shame. Idolatry — anything elevated to the place God should hold. Enmity and strife — sustained hostility and the habit of creating conflict. Jealousy and fits of anger — burning resentment and explosive rage. Rivalries and dissensions — self-promotion at others' expense, creating division where there should be unity. Envy — not just wanting what others have, but wanting them not to have it. Drunkenness — being controlled by substances rather than the Spirit.

From 2 Timothy 3

Lovers of self — making yourself the center of every consideration. Lovers of money — when financial security displaces God. Boastful and arrogant — self-promotion and contempt for others. Ungrateful — receiving grace as if it's owed. Without self-control — following every appetite without governance. Slanderers — using words to damage and destroy. Lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God — comfort and entertainment as the organizing principle of life. And then this devastating phrase: having the appearance of godliness but denying its power. Religious-looking people whose hearts are unchanged.

From Mark 7 — what Jesus said comes from the heart

Evil thoughts. Theft. Murder. Adultery. Coveting. Wickedness. Deceit — getting what you want through manipulation. Envy. Slander. Pride — the person who presents himself as above others. And foolishness — living as if God doesn't exist or doesn't matter.

James adds one more category: whoever knows the right thing to do and fails to do it — for him it is sin. Sins of omission count too. Not just what we've done, but what we've failed to do.
Reflect
Which items on those lists do you recognize in yourself — past or present? Not in general. Specifically. What would you name if you were being completely honest?
Don't move past this. Sit with it honestly.
Part Three
The Separation Sin Creates
Sin doesn't just make us feel guilty. It creates actual distance — a real rupture between us and God. Isaiah 59:2 says your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God. God doesn't disappear. Sin hides his face. The problem is on our side, not his.
This is the consequence that makes the cross necessary. Not that God is angry and needs to calm down — but that a holy God cannot simply overlook what sin actually is and what it actually costs. Death is the wage sin earns. Not as punishment handed down arbitrarily, but as the natural result of cutting yourself off from the source of life.
"And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked, following the course of this world."
Ephesians 2:1–2
Paul doesn't use death as a metaphor he's afraid to commit to. That's the condition. Dead. And that's exactly what makes the resurrection so significant — and what makes the next study, on the cross, the center of everything.
Reflect
Do you feel the separation Isaiah describes? When you pray, does it feel like God hears you? Is there something you've been holding onto that you already know creates distance between you and God?
Be honest with yourself here. No one else is listening.
This Week
The Challenge
Before You Finish
Go back through the sin lists — slowly, with a pen if you can. Mark every sin you recognize in your own life, past or present. Don't minimize. Don't rationalize. Just name it honestly before God. Come ready to talk about what you found.
"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting."
Psalm 139:23–24
That prayer — search me, know my heart — is the right posture for this study. Not self-flagellation. Not despair. Just openness. Let God show you what's there. That's the beginning of everything that comes next.
