Discipleship
"If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me."
Mark 8:34
Discipleship is one of those words that gets used so often in church contexts that it can lose its weight. By the time most people hear it, it means something like "being a good Christian" — vague and comfortable. Jesus had something far more specific in mind. And what he had in mind is what this study is about.
Part One
The Call Before the Explanation
Jesus didn't explain discipleship to the fishermen before he called them. He simply said: follow me, and I will make you fishers of men. They left their nets immediately. The pattern is worth noticing: the call came first. The formation came as they walked with him.
This is how discipleship works. It is not a program you complete before following. It is a life lived in the company of Jesus — being formed into his image as you go. The question is not "do I have enough information?" The question is: will I follow?
"If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me. For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will save it."
Luke 9:23–24
Reflect
When Jesus called the fishermen, they left immediately. Is there anything in your life right now that you're holding onto rather than leaving — something that's making you hesitate to follow fully?
Be specific. Don't answer in general terms.
Part Two
What Unconditional Means
Most people follow Jesus with conditions — things they're willing to give up, and things they're not. They follow in the areas where following is convenient or cheap. This isn't discipleship. It's brand loyalty. The word unconditional names what Jesus actually calls people to.
Jesus gives three requirements in Luke 9:23, and they escalate. First: deny yourself. Not as a spiritual discipline — as an ongoing posture. The self is not the center. Continually. Not once. Second: take up your cross. Not discomfort or inconvenience, but the willingness to be identified with a crucified criminal. Shame absorbed. Reputation risked. Daily. Third: follow me. Not follow a philosophy or a religion, but a person. Where he goes, you go. What he does, you do. What he says, you obey.
The paradox Jesus names in verse 24 is the heart of it all: whoever tries to save his life will lose it. Whoever loses his life for Jesus' sake will save it. The very thing we're trying to protect by holding back is the thing we forfeit by holding back. You don't get to keep yourself by refusing to surrender. You lose yourself faster.
Reflect
On a scale of honesty — what conditions are you currently placing on your discipleship? What are you still holding back? What would it cost you specifically to take that thing and lay it down?
Take a full minute here. Don't rush past this.
Part Three
The Apprentice Model
The word disciple in Greek is mathetes — it means learner or apprentice. Not a student in a classroom, but someone who follows a craftsman everywhere and learns by watching and doing. In the ancient world, a disciple was not primarily someone who knew what their teacher knew. They were someone who was becoming who their teacher was.
The goal of discipleship is not information — it is transformation. Romans 12:2 — be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Philippians 2:5 — let this mind be in you which was also in Christ Jesus. The disciple gradually looks more and more like the one they're following. Not through effort alone, but through sustained proximity — spending time with Jesus in his word, in prayer, in community, in obedience.
"All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you."
Matthew 28:18–20
Notice what Jesus says to teach: not to know everything, but to observe — to obey — everything. The measure of a disciple is not how much they know but how much they do. If you love me, Jesus says in John 14, you will keep my commandments. Obedience is the proof of relationship, not the path to it.
Part Four
What Discipleship Is Not
Discipleship is not attendance. Going to church, even faithfully, is not discipleship. It can be a context for discipleship — but the crowds that followed Jesus were not his disciples. His disciples followed him personally and were known by him personally.
Discipleship is not agreement. Believing the right things about Jesus is not discipleship. James 2 says even the demons believe — and shudder. Belief without obedience is not the faith Jesus calls people to.
Discipleship is not morality. Living a good, moral life is not discipleship. Many moral people are not following Jesus. The direction matters more than the current position — a person who is genuinely following Jesus and still messy is in a better position than a moral person who has never surrendered.
And discipleship is not comfortable. Jesus explicitly says the cross is the path. If discipleship is easy, something has been edited out.
Reflect
Which of those four — attendance, agreement, morality, comfort — has most described your version of following Jesus? What would actually following Jesus — surrendered, obedient, cross-carrying — look like differently in your life this week?
Be specific. Don't answer in general. Name something concrete.
This Week
The Challenge
Before You Finish
Identify one area of your life where you are following Jesus conditionally — where you've set a limit on what you'll surrender. Name it out loud, right now if you can. Then sit with Luke 9:23 to 24 and ask: what would unconditional obedience look like here — specifically, this week?
"Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven."
Matthew 7:21
Calling him Lord is not the same as treating him as Lord. The difference is obedience. That's discipleship. Not a program. Not a mood. A life of obedience to a person you actually trust.
