The Cross
"But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us."
Romans 5:8
This is the study at the center of everything. The cross is not just one topic among many — it is the event that makes every other truth in the Christian faith meaningful. The resurrection, forgiveness, adoption, purpose — they all trace back here.
Take your time with this one. You don't need to rush. The goal isn't to finish — it's to let the weight of this land. If something stops you, stay there. That's where the Spirit is working.
Part One
The Problem the Cross Solves
By now the problem is clear: sin has created a separation between humanity and God. The wages of sin is death — not as punishment from an angry God, but as the natural consequence of cutting yourself off from the source of life.
A just God cannot simply overlook sin. But a loving God cannot abandon the people he made. The cross is where justice and love meet.
"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."
John 3:16
The great exchange at the heart of the gospel: he became what we are so we could become what he is. He took our sin so we could receive his righteousness.
Reflect
Before going further — does the problem feel real to you? Not just intellectually — personally. Do you feel the weight of what sin has actually cost?
Pause and sit with this.
Part Two
What the Cross Accomplished
"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."
2 Corinthians 5:21
The cross accomplished five things — and each one is worth dwelling on separately.
Forgiveness. The debt of sin is paid. God can forgive without overlooking justice — because the penalty was fully borne by Jesus. Colossians 2:14 pictures the legal record of our debt being nailed to the cross and canceled.
Reconciliation. The separation Isaiah 59 describes is overcome. The wall between a holy God and sinful humanity is torn down. Access to God is restored — not as a right we earned, but as a gift we receive.
Redemption. We were slaves to sin. Jesus paid the price to buy us back — not with silver or gold, but with his life. The word ransom was used in the Roman world for the price that frees a slave.
Justification. God declares us not guilty — not because we're innocent, but because the penalty has been fully paid. Romans 5:1 — therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ.
Resurrection. Death is not the end. The resurrection is the Father's declaration that the sacrifice was accepted — and that death itself is defeated. A dead savior saves no one. The resurrection changes everything.
Reflect
If Jesus did all of this — specifically for you — what does that require of you? Not theologically. Personally. What would be an appropriate response to this?
Don't move on yet. Sit with that question.
Part Three
The Resurrection — Why Everything Depends on It
"And if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile and you are still in your sins. Then those also who have fallen asleep in Christ have perished. If in Christ we have hope in this life only, we are of all people most to be pitied."
1 Corinthians 15:17–19
Paul doesn't soften this: if the resurrection didn't happen, Christianity is a lie and Christians are fools. The resurrection is not a postscript to the cross. It is the Father's declaration that the sacrifice was accepted — and that death itself has been defeated.
The evidence for the resurrection is serious historical evidence. Here are six lines of it.
Evidence One
The Empty Tomb
All four gospels record the empty tomb. Neither the Jewish leaders nor the Roman authorities ever produced a body — despite having every motivation to do so. A body would have ended Christianity immediately. Instead, the authorities paid soldiers to claim the disciples stole it. They didn't deny the tomb was empty. They tried to explain it.
Evidence Two
Five Hundred Eyewitnesses
Paul's letter to the Corinthians — written within 20 years of the crucifixion — lists specific appearances: to Peter, to the Twelve, to more than 500 people at once. "Most of whom are still alive," Paul notes. That is not legend-language. It is an invitation to go ask them.
Evidence Three
The Disciples' Transformation
On the night of the arrest, every disciple fled. Peter denied Jesus three times. They hid behind locked doors in fear. Within weeks, these same men were publicly proclaiming the resurrection in Jerusalem — the city where it happened — and willing to die for it. People die for things they genuinely believe. Almost no one dies for something they know is a lie.
Evidence Four
Hostile Attestation
The earliest Jewish response to the resurrection claim was not "this is absurd — there was no empty tomb." It was "the disciples stole the body." This is your opponents conceding the facts while disputing the interpretation. The empty tomb was not contested by those with the most reason to contest it.
Evidence Five
The Conversion of James
During Jesus' ministry, his own brothers didn't believe in him. James is listed among the skeptics. After the resurrection, James becomes a leader of the Jerusalem church and is eventually martyred for his faith. Paul records a specific resurrection appearance to James. What changed his mind? He says: he saw his brother alive.
Evidence Six
The Conversion of Paul
Saul of Tarsus was not a confused agnostic. He was actively hunting and imprisoning Christians — convinced they were dangerous heretics. He was present at Stephen's execution. Then something happened on the road to Damascus that changed his entire life and eventually cost him his life. His account: he saw the risen Jesus.
Reflect
What do you make of this evidence? Is this something you could dismiss? If the resurrection actually happened — if Jesus is actually alive right now — what does that require of you?
Take your time here.
Part Four
What This Requires of You
"He died for all, that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who for their sake died and was raised."
2 Corinthians 5:15
The cross is not primarily information. It is an act — a sacrifice made, a debt paid, a death entered — on your behalf. If it is true, then it requires something. You cannot receive a gift of this magnitude and remain unchanged.
Reflection One
Before this study, what did the cross mean to you — if anything? How has that changed, or deepened, or been challenged today?
Take a full minute with this one.
Reflection Two
Romans 5:8 says that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. The initiative was entirely God's. What does it mean to you personally that you didn't have to earn this — that it was given before you deserved it?
Pause and sit with that.
Reflection Three
Second Corinthians 5:15 says those who receive what Christ did no longer live for themselves. What in your life would have to change if you took that seriously?
Be honest. Not theological — personal.

The Challenge
Before You Finish
Read Isaiah 53 slowly — the suffering servant, written 700 years before Jesus. Then read it again, inserting your own name where it says "our" and "we." Sit with what it means that this was done for you, personally. Not for humanity in general. For you.
"But he was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed."
Isaiah 53:5
That's the end of this study. But it's not really an end — it's an invitation. The cross stands open. What will you do with it?
