The Core Four · Pillar 01
Saturated in the Gospel
"Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle, set apart for the gospel of God… concerning his Son, Jesus Christ our Lord."
Romans 1:1–3
Ask most Christians what the gospel is and you'll get something like: God loves you, Jesus died for your sins, believe and go to heaven. That's not wrong — but it's incomplete. And incomplete gospels produce incomplete disciples. The first of the Core Four is about going back to the beginning and hearing what the gospel actually is.
Part One
The Royal Announcement
The word gospel — euangelion in Greek — means royal announcement. It's the word used in the Roman world when a general won a battle, or when a new king came to power. A herald would run ahead of the army and announce: the king has come. The battle is won. Everything has changed. That's what the gospel is.
"The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand; repent and believe in the gospel."
Mark 1:15 — Jesus' opening proclamation
Notice what Jesus didn't open with. He didn't open with "God loves you." He opened with a royal announcement: the Kingdom has arrived. Repent and believe the news. The gospel is the announcement that Jesus is King — and his Kingdom is now available to all who will enter it.
And that title — Christ — is not Jesus' last name. It is a title. The Greek translation of the Hebrew Messiah, meaning Anointed One. Anointing in the Old Testament commissioned kings, priests, and prophets. To call Jesus "Christ" is to declare him King, High Priest, and Prophet all in one. When Paul opens every letter "from Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus" — he is placing himself as a servant of a King.
Paul summarizes the gospel events in First Corinthians: Christ died for our sins. He was buried. He was raised on the third day. He appeared to more than 500 witnesses. And then — the part most people leave out — he ascended and is seated at the right hand of the Father. All authority in heaven and on earth belongs to him. He reigns. That's why it's good news rather than just a nice story.
Reflect
When you think about the gospel — what comes to mind first? Is it primarily what you receive, or is it primarily who Jesus is and what he's done? How might it change things if you led with the King rather than the benefits?
Take a moment with that.
Part Two
The Gospel vs. The Benefits
Here's where things go wrong. Most of what gets preached as "the gospel" is actually the benefits of the gospel — what you receive when you receive the King. These are real and wonderful: forgiveness of sins, adoption into God's family, the indwelling Holy Spirit, freedom from bondage, eternal life. These are true. These matter. But they are not the announcement itself.
The announcement is this: Jesus is King. The Kingdom has come. Death is defeated. The world is being restored. There is a new humanity. Everything has changed.
When we lead with the benefits and omit the announcement, we produce consumers — people who come to Jesus for what he gives rather than for who he is. The question "what do I get out of following Jesus?" is a very different starting point than "who is this King and what does his reign mean for everything?" The first produces a transaction. The second produces a disciple.
"Philip went down to the city of Samaria and proclaimed to them the Christ… But when they believed Philip as he preached good news about the kingdom of God and the name of Jesus Christ, they were baptized."
Acts 8:5, 12
Notice what Philip preached: the kingdom of God and the name of Jesus Christ. Both together. Not just the name — the kingdom. Not just benefits — the King's reign. That's the full gospel.
Reflect
If you're honest, has following Jesus felt more like a transaction — getting what you need — or more like allegiance to a King? What would it look like in your actual daily life to live as someone under the reign of Jesus rather than just the recipient of his gifts?
Sit with that honestly.
Part Three
What Saturated Means
The first of the Core Four is not merely "knows the gospel." It's saturated in it. The difference matters. A person who knows the gospel can recite it. A person saturated in the gospel is shaped by it — it is the lens through which they see everything else.
In identity, the gospel-saturated person knows they are defined by what God says, not by what they've done or what others think. Adoption replaces performance. Security replaces striving. In relationships, because they've received grace they didn't earn, they give grace they're not owed — forgiveness flowing from the gospel. In suffering, the resurrection means the worst thing is never the last thing — hope that isn't denial but deep confidence that God is at work. And in mission — people saturated in the gospel want others to have what they have. Acts 8:4 says those who were scattered went about preaching the word. The gospel announced produces people who announce the gospel.
"For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes."
Romans 1:16
The gospel is not a private comfort. It is the power of God — publicly announced. A saturated disciple lives that way: the announcement has changed everything, and they can't help but let it show.
Reflect
In which area of your life does the gospel feel most real right now — identity, relationships, suffering, or mission? Which area feels most disconnected from the gospel? What would it look like to bring the announcement of the King into that area this week?
Take your time here.
This Week
The Challenge
Before You Finish
Read Romans 1:1–17 slowly this week. Notice how Paul frames the gospel before he gets to sin, justification, or any of the theology we typically lead with. What comes first? What does that order tell you about what the gospel actually is?
"For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures."
1 Corinthians 15:3–4
These events — the death, the burial, the resurrection — are of first importance. Everything else in the Christian life flows from them. A disciple saturated in the gospel never gets far from these facts. They are not the beginning of the story. They are the center of it.
