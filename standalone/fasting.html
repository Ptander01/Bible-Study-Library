Resource Study · Spiritual Disciplines
Fasting
Not a diet, not a punishment — a reorientation of hunger toward God

Starting Here

Fasting is one of the most misunderstood practices in the Christian life. In some traditions it's treated as a form of spiritual achievement — something advanced disciples do to earn deeper access to God. In others it's been forgotten almost entirely, treated as an Old Testament relic with no place in the modern church. Neither of these gets it right.

Jesus assumes his disciples will fast. Not if you fast — when you fast (Matthew 6:16). He places it alongside prayer and generosity as one of the basic rhythms of the devoted life. And yet he also warns against using it as performance — a way to signal your seriousness to others while your heart stays untouched.

What is fasting, then? At its core, fasting is the voluntary abstention from something good — most commonly food — for a spiritual purpose. It is not about punishing the body. It is about interrupting the ordinary rhythms of physical life in order to become more attentive to God. The hunger you feel is a teacher. What it teaches, when you let it, is where your real trust lives.

"Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from the mouth of God."
Matthew 4:4 — Jesus in the wilderness, quoting Deuteronomy 8:3

Jesus spoke these words in the middle of a 40-day fast. Hungry, depleted, tempted to use his power for his own comfort — he chose instead to locate his life in the word of God rather than in bread. That is the heart of fasting: not the absence of food, but the presence of a deeper hunger being acknowledged and fed.

What Fasting Is Not

Not a Diet
The purpose is entirely spiritual. Weight loss or health benefits may occur, but pursuing them is not fasting in the biblical sense. The moment physical benefit becomes the goal, the practice has become something else.
Not Earning God
Fasting doesn't obligate God to act. It doesn't accumulate spiritual credit. God is not more impressed with you because you skipped meals. He is not swayed by suffering — he is moved by a humble, seeking heart, which fasting is designed to cultivate.
Not Performance
Jesus is explicit: if you fast to be seen, you have your reward — human recognition — and that's all you'll get. The fast that transforms is invisible to everyone except God. Announcing your fast or making sure people notice your discipline corrupts the purpose.
Not Self-Punishment
Fasting is not penance — suffering to atone for sin. That work is finished. The cross is sufficient. Fasting is not about making yourself hurt enough to deserve forgiveness. It is about clearing space so that grace, which is already available, can be more fully received.
Not Only Food
Scripture focuses primarily on fasting from food, but the underlying principle — voluntary abstention from something good for a spiritual purpose — extends to anything that competes for your attention and affection. The Puritans wrote about fasting from entertainment, comfort, and distraction for this reason.

What Fasting Actually Does

This is where the theology of fasting becomes personal. The physical act of not eating does several things in the spiritual life that are difficult to achieve any other way.

Reveals Hunger
When you fast, you discover what you actually depend on. Most people are surprised to find how much of their mood, their sense of stability, and their moment-to-moment comfort rests on food — or on whatever they're fasting from. That discovery is itself the point. You cannot address a dependency you haven't named.
Sharpens Prayer
Throughout Scripture, fasting and prayer are almost always paired. The hunger creates urgency. The discomfort keeps you present. The absence of one comfort drives you toward another — the presence of God. Many people who fast find that prayer becomes less rote and more genuine simply because their body is asking for something and they redirect that asking toward God.
Reorients Desire
We live in a culture of immediate satisfaction. Every appetite is catered to instantly. Fasting is an act of counter-formation — deliberately practicing the experience of wanting something and not having it, in order to train your desires toward what lasts. It loosens the grip of the ordinary so something deeper can be grasped.
Expresses Urgency
In Scripture, fasting often accompanies crisis, repentance, or a desperate need for God's intervention. It is the body's way of saying: this matters enough to set aside ordinary life. When words feel insufficient — when the need is too large for a quiet prayer — fasting gives the whole person a way to express what the heart is carrying.
Cultivates Humility
Hunger is humbling. It is a physical reminder that you are a creature, not the Creator — that you are dependent, not self-sufficient. In a culture that prizes control and self-reliance, the experience of genuine need is spiritually clarifying. Ezra 8:21 — "I proclaimed a fast… that we might humble ourselves before our God."

Who Fasted — and Why

Fasting runs through the entire biblical narrative. It is not a marginal practice — it appears at almost every major turning point in the story of God's people.

Moses
Receiving the Law
Exodus 34:28 · Deuteronomy 9:9
40 days without food or water — twice. Once when receiving the Law, and again after the golden calf incident when he interceded for Israel. Moses' fasting was inseparable from his intimacy with God. "The Lord spoke to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend."
David
Intercession & Grief
2 Samuel 12:16 · Psalm 35:13
David fasted for his dying child — not as a transaction ("fast enough and God changes his mind") but as an expression of desperate love and dependence. He also fasted for his enemies when they were ill. Fasting as intercession: the body entering into prayer at the deepest level.
Elijah
Exhaustion & Renewal
1 Kings 19:4–8
After Carmel, Elijah collapses in the wilderness — burned out, suicidal. God's response is not a rebuke but bread and water: "The journey is too great for you." Then 40 days to Horeb. Sometimes fasting isn't chosen — it's the desert God walks you through before the still small voice.
Esther
Corporate Crisis
Esther 4:15–16
"Fast for me. Do not eat or drink for three days, night or day. I and my young women will also fast." Corporate fasting in the face of catastrophic threat — the survival of an entire people. "And if I perish, I perish." Fasting as the community's way of throwing itself entirely on God before an outcome they cannot control.
Daniel
Intercession & Revelation
Daniel 9:3 · Daniel 10:2–3
Daniel fasted in sackcloth and ashes while confessing the sins of Israel and praying for restoration. In chapter 10, a 21-day partial fast preceded a profound vision. Fasting as sustained, devoted intercession for people and purposes beyond yourself.
Jesus
Before Public Ministry
Matthew 4:1–11 · Luke 4:1–13
40 days in the wilderness before his public ministry began. The Spirit led him there — fasting was not incidental to preparation, it was the preparation. Every temptation Satan offered was aimed at Jesus' physical need. Every answer came from the word. The fast was the training ground for a ministry that would not be driven by appetite.
The Early Church
Mission & Leadership
Acts 13:1–3 · Acts 14:23
Before sending Paul and Barnabas on the first missionary journey, the church at Antioch worshiped and fasted. Before appointing elders in every church, Paul and Barnabas prayed and fasted. Major spiritual decisions in the early church were accompanied by fasting — not as a requirement but as a natural expression of dependence on God's direction.

What to Expect When You Fast

Honest preparation matters. Many people fast once, have a difficult experience, and never try again. Often the difficulty was normal — they just didn't know what to expect.

The First Few Hours

Hunger is real and at first it is distracting rather than clarifying. Your body is accustomed to eating at certain intervals and will remind you insistently. This is normal. Don't mistake the first wave of discomfort for spiritual dryness — it's just your body adjusting. Most people find that the first few hours are the hardest, and that a genuine sense of clarity and attentiveness to God often comes later.

What Often Surfaces

Fasting has a way of revealing what's been suppressed. Emotions, anxieties, grievances, and longings that were being managed by comfort and distraction tend to surface when the distraction is removed. Don't be alarmed — this is part of the point. Bring what surfaces to God in prayer rather than rushing to end the fast to make it stop.

Irritability is also common, especially early. Be gentle with yourself and with those around you. If you're fasting regularly, those who live with you probably need to know — not so you get credit, but so your mood is understood in context.

Drink Water

Unless you are doing a very short fast under careful spiritual direction, drink water throughout. The biblical fasts of 40 days without food or water (Moses, Jesus) are extraordinary and not normative. Dehydration creates physical symptoms — headache, confusion, fatigue — that are not spiritually productive. Water is not breaking the fast.

The Reward Jesus Promises

"And your Father who sees in secret will reward you."
Matthew 6:18

Jesus doesn't say fasting might be rewarded, or that it could be rewarded under the right conditions. He says: your Father will reward you. The reward is not specified — and that's deliberate. The reward is God himself, encountered more fully in the space that fasting creates. It is less like a transaction and more like clearing away furniture so you can finally see the room you've been living in.

First Step
Before planning an extended fast, begin with one meal. Skip lunch one day this week. During that time — instead of eating — pray. Take what the hunger says and redirect it toward God. See what surfaces. That single meal is a real fast, and it will teach you more than reading about fasting ever will.

Reflection Questions

Have you ever fasted before? What was your experience — what did it reveal about where your comfort and stability actually come from?
Jesus says "when you fast" — assuming his followers will. Does that assumption match how you've thought about fasting? What has kept you from it, if anything?
Is there a situation in your life right now — a decision, a burden, a relationship, a need — that feels weighty enough to fast over? What would it look like to bring your whole body into that prayer?
Isaiah 58 connects fasting to justice — caring for the poor, freeing the oppressed, housing the homeless. How does that expand or challenge your understanding of what fasting is for?
Practical Guide
Practices
Types of fasting, how to prepare, and how to use the time well
⚕
A note on health: If you have diabetes, a history of eating disorders, are pregnant or nursing, take medications that require food, or have any condition affecting blood sugar or metabolism — consult your doctor before fasting from food. The spiritual discipline is real; so is your body. Fasting is not intended to harm. If food fasting isn't medically appropriate for you, consider a media or entertainment fast instead — the spiritual principle is the same.

Types of Fasting in Scripture

Normal Fast
Matthew 4:2 · Luke 4:2
Abstaining from all food but drinking water. The most common biblical fast. Can range from a single meal to multiple days. Sustainable for most people in good health and the recommended starting point for those new to the practice.
Partial Fast
Daniel 10:2–3 · Daniel 1:12
Restricting certain foods rather than all food. Daniel abstained from "delicacies, meat, and wine" for 21 days. Appropriate when a full fast isn't medically possible, or when a longer sustained period of focused prayer is the goal.
Complete Fast
Esther 4:16 · Acts 9:9
No food or water for a brief, defined period — typically no more than three days. Esther's three-day fast before approaching the king. Paul's three days without food or water after encountering Jesus on the road to Damascus. Not recommended without spiritual direction and medical awareness.
Corporate Fast
Joel 2:15–16 · Esther 4:16 · Acts 13:2
The whole community fasting together — for repentance, intercession, or a major decision. Some of the most powerful examples in Scripture are corporate: Joel's call to Israel, Esther's three days with all the Jews in Susa, the Antioch church before Paul's first journey.
Media / Comfort Fast
Principle from Isaiah 58
Abstaining from entertainment, social media, news, or comfort for a defined period. Not directly named in Scripture but rooted in the same principle — removing something that occupies attention and appetite in order to redirect that attention toward God. Often powerfully clarifying in the digital age.
Sunrise to Sunset
Judges 20:26 · 2 Samuel 1:12
Fasting from first light to sunset — the traditional pattern of the Jewish Day of Atonement and many historical fasts. A natural rhythm that doesn't require sleeping through hunger and is sustainable for regular practice. Eat breakfast early, break the fast at sundown.

How to Prepare

I
Define the Purpose Before You Begin
A fast without a purpose is just skipping meals

Before you fast, answer: what is this for? A decision you need clarity on? Intercession for someone in crisis? Repentance and a fresh start? Preparation for something significant? Spiritual breakthrough in an area where you feel stuck?

Write it down. Name it specifically. The written purpose becomes the anchor for every prayer during the fast — and when hunger hits hard, returning to "this is why I'm here" is what keeps the fast from becoming mere discomfort.

II
Start Smaller Than You Think You Should
One meal is a real fast

The most common mistake is beginning with an ambitious multi-day fast and abandoning it in misery by hour six. This creates discouragement and makes the next attempt less likely. Start with one meal — skip lunch, spend that hour in prayer. Then try a sunrise-to-sunset fast. Build the practice before extending it.

John Wesley fasted every Wednesday and Friday until 3pm for much of his ministry — not occasionally, but as a regular rhythm. Consistency over time matters more than intensity in a single attempt.

III
Plan What You'll Do With the Time
The absence of food creates space — fill it intentionally

A fast without a plan for the time easily becomes just hunger. Every meal you'd normally eat represents 20–40 minutes of time that is now available. What will you do with it?

  • Pray specifically — return to the written purpose and pray through it in detail
  • Read Scripture — particularly passages related to your purpose; the hunger sharpens attention
  • Journal — what is surfacing? What is God saying? What are you noticing about your own heart?
  • Sit in silence — do nothing. Ask God to speak. Wait. The discomfort of doing nothing is itself part of the formation.
  • Worship — music, psalms read aloud, confession and gratitude expressed vocally
IV
Break the Fast Gently
How you end matters as much as how you begin

After a full-day or multi-day fast, don't immediately eat a large meal — your digestive system has been resting and needs to re-engage gradually. Start with something light: broth, fruit, a small amount of easily digestible food. Give it an hour before eating normally.

The breaking of the fast is also a spiritual moment. Express gratitude. Ask what you received. The Lord's Supper in the early church was often preceded by fasting — eating together after was a celebration. Let the return to eating be a conscious act of worship rather than just relief.

Isaiah 58 — The Fast God Chooses

This is the most important passage on fasting in the entire Bible, and the most challenging. Israel is fasting — technically, religiously, correctly — and God rejects it. Not because fasting is wrong, but because their fasting is disconnected from how they treat people.

"Is not this the fast that I choose: to loose the bonds of wickedness, to undo the straps of the yoke, to let the oppressed go free, and to break every yoke? Is it not to share your bread with the hungry and bring the homeless poor into your house; when you see the naked, to cover him, and not to hide yourself from your own flesh?"
Isaiah 58:6–7

The fast God chooses is not primarily about what you don't eat. It is about a posture of open-handedness toward the poor, the oppressed, and the vulnerable. A person who fasts from food while remaining closed to the needs around them has missed the point entirely. The discipline of the body is meant to produce an openness of the hands.

This doesn't make fasting from food optional — Isaiah 58 assumes that fasting is a real and valuable practice. But it insists that fasting be accompanied by justice, generosity, and genuine care for others. The hunger you feel during a fast is a small window into what chronic hunger actually is. Let it move you toward those who are always hungry.

Word Study
Fast · Hunger · Humility
What the original words reveal about the nature and purpose of fasting
νηστεύω
nēsteuō
verb — Greek
To fast; to abstain from food; to go without eating. From nē (not) + esthiō (to eat). The standard New Testament word for fasting. Used by Jesus in Matthew 6:16–17, by the disciples of John who note Jesus' disciples don't fast (Matthew 9:14), and by the early church in Acts 13 and 14.
The noun form nēsteia (fast, fasting) appears in Acts 27:9 referring to the Day of Atonement — the most solemn fast in the Jewish calendar. Paul uses it in 2 Corinthians 6:5 and 11:27 listing it among the hardships of apostolic ministry. The word is entirely neutral — it describes the act; the spiritual meaning comes from the intention behind it.
צוּם
tsûm
verb — Hebrew
To fast; to abstain from food. The primary Old Testament word for fasting. Appears in the context of mourning (2 Samuel 1:12), intercession (2 Samuel 12:16), repentance (Joel 1:14), and preparation for divine encounter (Exodus 34:28). Used of individuals, groups, and the entire nation.
The associated noun tsom (fast) appears in Isaiah 58:5 — "Is such the fast that I choose?" God's question in that passage is not whether fasting is valid, but whether it is accompanied by the right orientation of heart and action. The word itself carries no inherent spiritual weight — tsûm is what you do; what it produces in you is the question.
עָנָה
anah
verb — Hebrew
To humble oneself; to afflict; to be brought low. Closely associated with fasting throughout the Old Testament. In Leviticus 16:29, the command for the Day of Atonement is to "afflict yourselves" (anah) — the context makes clear this means fasting. In Psalm 35:13, David says "I afflicted myself with fasting." The word captures fasting's essential posture: self-humbling before God.
The connection between fasting and humility is not incidental — it is the heart of the practice. Anah describes what happens to the person who fasts genuinely: they are brought low, made aware of their dependence, stripped of the ordinary props of comfort and self-sufficiency. Isaiah 58:3 records the people saying "we have humbled ourselves (anah)" — and God's response is to question whether their humility extended to how they treated others. Humility before God and humility toward people cannot be separated.
πεινάω
peinaō
verb — Greek
To hunger; to be hungry; to crave food. The physical experience of wanting food that fasting deliberately creates. Jesus uses this word in the Beatitudes: "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied" (Matthew 5:6). He uses it of himself after the 40-day fast in Matthew 4:2: "And after fasting forty days and forty nights, he was hungry."
The link between physical hunger (peinaō) and spiritual hunger is not metaphorical in the Beatitudes — it is deliberate. The person who knows what it is to physically hunger and who redirects that impulse toward righteousness has learned something about desire itself. Fasting trains the appetite: you practice wanting one thing, feeling that want, and turning toward something better. Over time, the direction of hunger can change.
ἀποδίδωμι
apodidōmi
verb — Greek
To give back; to repay; to render; to reward. The word Jesus uses in Matthew 6:18 — "your Father who sees in secret will reward (apodidōmi) you." It carries the sense of rendering what is due — a return for something given. Not an optional bonus, but an appropriate response to what has been offered.
Jesus uses the same word for the reward of secret prayer (6:6) and secret generosity (6:4) — the three hidden disciplines of Matthew 6 all carry the same promise. Apodidōmi implies that the Father takes genuine account of what is done in secret. The fast that no one sees is not wasted — it is seen, and it will be returned with something appropriate. The reward is not specified because the reward is God himself, and what he gives cannot be itemized in advance.
