Self-Assessment
Where Are You?
Twenty honest questions — not to put you in a box, but to show you the next step

Growth isn't optional in the Christian life — it's the norm. A healthy baby grows. If a baby stops growing, something is wrong. The same is true spiritually. Hebrews 5 describes believers who'd been Christians long enough to be teachers, but were still drinking milk.

This isn't a personality test, and it's not meant to shame you. It's meant to do one thing: tell you the truth about where you actually are right now, so you — and whoever is discipling you — know what's actually needed next.

How to Use This

Read every statement honestly — not how you wish you were, but how you actually are right now. Check every box that's true of you. There are no wrong answers, only honest and dishonest ones. Then see where you land.

Same Seed, Different Soil

Before you read too much into your stage, consider something Jesus already taught about exactly this. In the parable of the sower, one seed lands in four different soils and produces wildly different results — not because the seed changed, but because the soil did. The seed was never the variable. The conditions were.

Same seed. Same time in the ground. Different soil.
"Other seed fell on good soil and produced grain, growing up and increasing and yielding thirtyfold and sixtyfold and a hundredfold... The seed sown on good soil refers to someone who hears the word and understands it. They produce a crop."
Mark 4:8, Matthew 13:23

The soil, in the spiritual life, isn't mysterious — it's specific, namable conditions. Here are five of the biggest:

  • An actual discipler. Not just attending the same church as mature believers — someone specifically, intentionally investing in your growth.
  • Real teaching exposure. Substantive instruction in Scripture, not just inspirational content consumed passively.
  • Trials processed with someone. Hard seasons either get worked through in community or get survived alone and unprocessed — those produce very different outcomes.
  • Responsibility given before feeling ready. Being asked to serve, lead, or teach is often what forces the next stage of growth, not a reward for already having arrived.
  • Growing people nearby. Who you're closest to shapes your ceiling — comfortable, stagnant friendships rarely produce growth either of you didn't already have.

The Framework
The Five Stages
Spiritually Dead · Infant · Child · Young Adult · Parent

Scripture describes spiritual growth the same way it describes physical growth — as a normal, expected progression, not an optional upgrade for the unusually devoted. Paul, the writer of Hebrews, and John all use the language of family development — infants, children, young men, fathers — to describe where a believer actually is, not just where they'd like to think they are.

1
Spiritually Dead
Not yet born again — not a "bad Christian," but not yet a Christian at all. This stage is marked by unbelief, not behavior. Some people here look very moral. Some look nothing like it. The marker isn't outward conduct — it's the absence of a real relationship with Christ.
"And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked, following the course of this world."
Ephesians 2:1–2
2
Spiritual Infant
Newly born again. Often excited, sometimes dramatically changed, but still almost entirely dependent. An infant doesn't feed itself — someone else has to do the feeding. The danger here isn't immaturity itself; every believer starts here. The danger is staying here.
"I fed you with milk, not solid food, for you were not ready for it. And even now you are not yet ready."
1 Corinthians 3:1–2
3
Spiritual Child
Knows the language of faith now. Can be genuinely excited, even endearing — and also rebellious, self-centered, and prone to doing the right thing only when there's a reward or punishment attached. Correction tends to provoke a reaction here: defensiveness or withdrawal rather than reflection.
"As you know, we dealt with each of you as a father deals with his own children, encouraging, comforting and urging you to live lives worthy of God."
1 Thessalonians 2:11–12
4
Spiritual Young Adult
Looking much more like a mature believer. Increasingly independent in their walk with God, eager to serve, capable of discerning right from wrong without someone walking them through every decision. Real blind spots remain — usually around self-focus — but there's growing awareness of them.
"But solid food is for the mature, who because of practice have their powers of discernment trained to distinguish good from evil."
Hebrews 5:14
5
Spiritual Parent
No longer primarily focused on their own growth — joy and energy are now genuinely invested in the growth of others. Able to teach, able to give counsel from Scripture, and intentional about training the next generation of disciple-makers rather than just gathering disciples around themselves.
"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
2 Timothy 2:2

A Warning Against Staying Put

The audience in Hebrews 5 wasn't new. They'd been believers long enough that they ought to have been teachers by then. Instead, they still needed someone to re-teach them the basics. Time in the faith doesn't automatically produce growth in the faith — intention does. A man can sit in the same pew for twenty years and still be functioning as a spiritual infant, if no one has ever pushed him past it and he's never pushed himself.

Mature in Years, Immature in Spirit

Physical age and spiritual age run on two different clocks. It's entirely possible to be forty, married, employed, and still functioning as a spiritual child underneath all of it. None of these signs are about feelings on a hard day — they're patterns. Read them as a mirror, not an accusation.

Relational Signs

You have few or no relationships where someone actually knows what's going on inside you, beyond surface updates.

Conflict makes you withdraw, attack, or perform — rarely do you stay present, calm, and honest in the room.

Your apologies are usually "sorry you feel that way" instead of naming specifically what you did.

Your friendships are built mostly around shared activities, not shared depth.

Character Signs

Your private life looks meaningfully different from the version of you people see in public.

Under stress, your first instinct is self-protection or self-justification, not honesty.

Compliments and criticism swing your mood disproportionately — your sense of self leans heavily on others' opinions.

You rarely initiate your own confession or correction; you wait to get caught instead of coming forward.

Spiritual Signs

Your devotional life depends almost entirely on circumstances — crisis drives you to pray, calm lets it lapse.

You can recite the right biblical answer but rarely let Scripture actually interrupt a decision you've already made.

You haven't been meaningfully corrected by anyone in years — not because you're doing great, but because you've made yourself hard to correct.

Worship and prayer feel like a box to check rather than an actual encounter with God.

If Several of These Landed

That's not a verdict — it's information. Go back to the wheel above and the assessment in the first tab. The point was never to feel bad about where you are. It's to stop being vague about it, so you can actually do something about it.

The Framework, Part Two
Four Spheres
Maturity isn't one number — it's measured in four places at once

Stage tells you roughly where someone is. Sphere tells you where that's actually true. A man can be a spiritual young adult in his private devotion to God and still be functioning like a spiritual child at home. Don't just ask "what stage am I" — ask "what stage am I, in each of these four places."

Relationship With God
Is your devotion to God growing — Scripture, prayer, obedience — or has it gone flat? This is the root sphere; the other three flow out of this one. A man can fake the other three for a while. He can't fake this one for long without it showing.
Relationship With the Church
Are you genuinely connected to a body of believers, or just attending? Mature disciples are committed to specific people — not just a building or a service time. Isolation is one of the clearest signs of stalled growth.
Relationship at Home
Often the most honest measure of all four. It's entirely possible to look mature at church and be far less mature with your spouse, your kids, or your own household. Maturity that doesn't show up at home isn't maturity yet — it's a performance.
Relationship With the World
How do you engage people who don't yet follow Christ? A mature disciple is salt and light outward, not just inward — present and active in the world without being absorbed by it. Retreating entirely from non-believers isn't holiness; it's hiding.
Worth Asking

Which of these four would the people closest to you say is your weakest right now — not which one you'd pick for yourself, but which one they'd pick if they were being honest?

The Goal
Becoming a Parent
Multiplication, not just maturity

The goal of this whole framework was never just "grow up." It's multiplication. Paul's instruction to Timothy contains four generations in a single sentence — and that's the model, not the exception.

"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
2 Timothy 2:2

Paul → Timothy → faithful men → others also. Discipleship was never meant to stop at "me growing." It was meant to keep going through you, to someone else, who keeps it going through someone else again.

The Math of Multiplication

If every believer just adds — brings people to church, encourages a few people — growth is linear and slow. If every believer multiplies — trains people who go on to train others — growth compounds. A church full of spiritual children being cared for forever stays the same size. A church full of spiritual parents training other parents grows without limit. The entire point of this framework is to move people toward Stage Five, not to keep them comfortable at Stage Two or Three.

What Keeps People From Becoming Parents

Fear of not knowing enough

Waiting to feel like an expert before discipling anyone. Most disciple-making isn't expert teaching — it's one step ahead, sharing what you actually know.

Comfort with being cared for

It's genuinely easier to stay the one being discipled than to become the one discipling. Staying a child indefinitely is comfortable. It's also a dead end.

A church culture that produces consumers

Some churches are built to deliver content to attenders rather than develop disciple-makers. If the system never asks anything of you, it will never produce a parent out of you.

No one ever asked

Sometimes the only thing missing is an actual invitation — someone naming you specifically and asking you to invest in someone else. Don't wait to be asked forever. Ask God who, and ask that person this week.

"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you."
Matthew 28:19–20
Application
Reflection & Discussion
For personal honesty, and for the room

Personal Reflection

Looking honestly at the assessment, what stage best describes you right now — and does that match what you assumed about yourself before you started?
In which of the four spheres — God, church, home, world — are you most mature? Which is weakest, and why do you think that is?
Who is currently discipling you? If no one is, what's actually kept you from asking someone?
Who could you begin investing in this month, even informally? What's stopped you so far?

Group Discussion Questions

  • Before tonight, would you have said you were growing, or just attending? What's the actual difference between those two things?
  • Hebrews 5:12 describes people who'd been believers long enough to be teachers but still needed milk. How does someone get stuck like that for years without realizing it?
  • Which of the five stages do you think is the easiest to get permanently stuck in, and why?
  • A healthy disciple-maker trains people to disciple others, not just to depend on them. Where have you seen dependency mistaken for discipleship — in yourself or in someone else?
  • What would it actually look like, practically, for you to move one stage forward in the next ninety days?
