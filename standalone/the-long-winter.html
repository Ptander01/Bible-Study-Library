The Long Season
You Are Not Lost
What it actually feels like to be in a long hard season — and why the numbness, the tiredness, and the distance from God are not signs that something has gone wrong with your faith

You are probably not here because things just got hard. You are here because things have been hard for a while — long enough that the initial crisis adrenaline wore off, long enough that the people around you have moved on to other concerns, long enough that you stopped being able to explain to yourself why it isn't getting better yet. The hard thing is still here. And you are tired in the specific way that only a long season produces: not just physically tired, but tired of being tired. Tired of adjusting. Tired of the gap between the life you expected and the one you are actually living.

This study is for that place. Not for the acute crisis — that needs different things. This is for the long middle, where the drama has subsided and what's left is just the dailyness of a hard thing that isn't resolving. The gray. The numb. The prayers that feel like they're going nowhere. The version of yourself that used to function differently, and doesn't anymore, and you don't know if that version is coming back.

What You Are Probably Experiencing

Long seasons tend to produce a recognizable set of internal experiences. Not everyone has all of them, but most people in a sustained hard season will recognize most of this list:

Numbness
Not depression exactly — more like the emotional volume has been turned down. You can still feel things, but everything requires more effort to reach. Worship doesn't move you the way it used to. Good things feel muted. You go through the motions without the sensation that used to accompany them.
Distance From God
Not dramatic doubt or angry unbelief — just quiet. Prayer feels like talking into a room where you can't tell if anyone is listening. The sense of God's presence that used to feel accessible feels like something you have to work to find now, and sometimes the working doesn't produce it.
Cognitive and Physical Fog
Your brain isn't working the way it should, whether from illness, exhaustion, grief, or all three. Reading is harder. Concentration is harder. The things that used to come easily now require effort that depletes you faster than they should. You are not imagining this. It is real, and it is part of what the season costs.
Loss of the Former Self
You remember a version of yourself who had more energy, more capacity, more joy — and you don't know if that version is still in there or if it has been replaced permanently by this one. The grief of that loss is real and rarely gets named. You are not just grieving your circumstances. You are grieving yourself.
The Pull Toward Escape
Something — screen time, food, sexual release, alcohol, isolation, anything that offers a few minutes of not feeling this — has a stronger pull than it normally does. This is not a character failure. It is a sign that you are depleted and your nervous system is looking for relief. It becomes a problem when the escape starts costing you things that matter more than the relief is worth.
Guilt About the Struggle
You know people have it worse. You know you are loved. You know the right things theologically. And you still feel this way — and then you feel guilty for feeling this way, which adds weight to an already heavy load. The guilt is not helpful. It is also not a sign of ingratitude. It is what happens when a person who cares about their faith can't seem to produce the faith they think they should have.

This Is Not the Absence of Faith

The most important thing to say at the beginning is this: what you are experiencing is not evidence that your faith has failed. The numbness, the distance, the fog, the pull toward escape — none of these are the spiritual state of a man who has lost his faith. They are the spiritual state of a man who is in a long hard season and is still here.

The Psalms were written by people who felt exactly what you are feeling. Psalm 42 is not a psalm of triumph — it is a psalm of a man whose soul is cast down, who is taunted by people asking where his God is, who is crying day and night, who is telling himself to hope in God while clearly not feeling the hope. He was not an unbeliever. He was a man of deep faith in a hard season, writing honestly about what that season felt like from the inside.

"Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation and my God."
Psalm 42:11 · ESV

He said "I shall again praise him" — future tense. He was not praising in that moment. He was telling himself to hold on. That is not weak faith. That is faith doing exactly what faith does in a long winter: it holds on without feeling like holding on. It says "I shall again" when the present moment doesn't have the again in it yet.

You are not lost. You are in the middle of something. Those are different.

You don't have to feel better to pray. You don't have to have the right words or the right emotional state. The psalmist's prayer started with "my soul is cast down" — and then turned toward God anyway. That turn is the prayer. Start there. Tell God where you are. He already knows. But you need to say it.
The Psalms of Descent
How to Grieve Well
What lament is, why the Bible is full of it, and how to bring your grief to God without either suppressing it or spiraling in it

The Permission You May Not Have Been Given

Much of Christian culture handles suffering by moving as quickly as possible toward the resolution. Acknowledge the hard thing, apply the appropriate scripture, identify the lesson, move toward gratitude. The process is not wrong in itself — there is a time for all of those things. But it often skips something the Bible does not skip: the honest, extended, unresolved naming of grief in the presence of God. That is what lament is. And the Psalms — one third of which are lament psalms — suggest that God not only permits this but invited it.

Lament is not the same as despair. Despair has given up on God. Lament is still talking to God — still oriented toward him — but from the hard place, without pretending to be somewhere else. The lament psalms do not resolve neatly. Psalm 88 ends in darkness with no resolution. That psalm is in the Bible. God preserved it. He did not edit out the ending.

The Structure of Biblical Lament

The lament psalms follow a recognizable pattern that can be used as a framework for your own honest prayer in a long season.

1
Address — Turn Toward God
Even in the hard place, the psalmist turned toward God rather than away. "O God." "My God." "Lord." The address is the act of faith — it says: I am bringing this to you, not away from you. You start where you are, not where you think you should be.
2
Complaint — Name What Is Actually True
The psalmists named their pain directly and specifically. "My tears have been my food day and night." "I am a worm, not a man." "Why have you forsaken me?" The complaint is not faithless — it is honest. God does not need you to protect him from the reality of your experience. Name it. All of it.
3
Ask — Bring the Specific Request
"Hear me." "Do not be far from me." "Restore to me the joy of your salvation." The lament psalms ask specifically — not generically hoping God will do something, but naming what is needed. You are allowed to ask for what you actually want. God already knows. Asking is for your sake as much as his.
4
Remember — Call Up What You Know Is True
"Yet you are holy." "I remember the days of old." "Your steadfast love never ceases." The psalmist turned from the present experience to the record of who God has been — not to cancel out the complaint, but to anchor it. You are not trying to feel better. You are reminding yourself of what is true regardless of how you feel.
5
Trust — Hold On Without Resolution
"I shall again praise him." "My hope is in you." "Though he slay me, yet will I hope in him." The trust at the end of a lament psalm is not triumphant. It is quiet, deliberate, sometimes barely there. It does not require the feeling of trust — only the choice of it. "I don't feel this, but I am choosing it" is valid. It is in the Bible.

Psalm 42 — A Lament to Read Slowly

Psalm 42:1–8 · ESV
1As a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God.

2My soul thirsts for God, for the living God. When shall I come and appear before God?

3My tears have been my food day and night, while they say to me all the day long, "Where is your God?"

4These things I remember, as I pour out my soul: how I would go with the throng and lead them in procession to the house of God with glad shouts and songs of praise, a multitude keeping festival.

5Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation and my God.

6My soul is cast down within me; therefore I remember you.

7Deep calls to deep at the roar of your waterfalls; all your breakers and your waves have gone over me.

8By day the Lord commands his steadfast love, and at night his song is with me, a prayer to the God of my life.

Notice what verse 6 does: "My soul is cast down within me; therefore I remember you." The therefore is doing unexpected work. The soul being cast down is the reason for turning toward God — not the obstacle to it. He is not saying "despite my soul being cast down, I remember you." He is saying the downcast state itself is what drives him to remember. The hard place is the reason to turn toward God, not the reason to wait until you feel better to do it.

Spiraling vs. Lamenting — The Difference

Lament has a direction: it moves toward God. Spiraling moves inward and loops. The diagnostic is simple: after you have spent time with what is hard, are you more oriented toward God or less? Lament leaves you anchored differently than it found you — not necessarily feeling better, but more connected to the one who holds the season. Spiraling leaves you more exhausted, more isolated, more fixated on the pain. Both involve feeling the hard thing. Only one of them has somewhere to go with it.

Address God, name what is actually true, ask for what you specifically need, remember something you know is true about him, and choose trust even if you can't feel it. A few sentences at each step is enough.

The Anchors
What Holds
The things that are true regardless of how you feel — not to override the feeling, but to give you something to hold onto when the feeling is all there is

In a long season, feelings are not reliable guides. Not because feelings are unimportant — they are real, and this study takes them seriously. But a man in the long middle cannot navigate by his emotional state, because his emotional state is depleted, fogged, and sometimes actively misleading. He needs something that holds independent of how he feels. These are those things.

These are not meant to be read as a list of things you should feel. They are meant to be read as a list of things that are true. Truth and feeling are not the same thing. In a long winter, you hold onto the truth and wait for the feeling to catch up. Sometimes it doesn't catch up for a long time. You hold on anyway.

You Are Held
Isaiah 43:2
"When you pass through the waters, I will be with you; and through the rivers, they shall not overwhelm you."
God did not say the waters would not come. He said they would not overwhelm. You are in the water. The promise is not absence of the water — it is presence in it. He is in this season with you, not waiting on the other side of it.
Nothing Can Separate
Romans 8:38–39
"Neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus."
"Things present" is on the list. The current season — this specific hard thing in the present moment — is named and cannot separate you from his love. The distance you feel is not distance he has created. The love is still operative even when it is not felt.
He Is Acquainted
Hebrews 4:15
"For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin."
Jesus is not observing your suffering from a distance. He is acquainted with it from the inside — with exhaustion, with the Father feeling silent (Gethsemane), with physical limitation, with the weight of a hard thing that had to be gone through. He knows what you are in.
Weakness Is Not Disqualifying
2 Corinthians 12:9
"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness."
Paul begged three times to have the hard thing removed. God said no, and gave him this instead. The weakness you are experiencing is not a problem God needs you to overcome before he can work. It is the condition he specifically said he works through. You do not have to be well to be used. You do not have to be strong to be held.
The Suffering Is Not Waste
Romans 5:3–5
"We rejoice in our sufferings, knowing that suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope."
Paul was not celebrating the pain. He was naming what it is producing — even when the production is invisible from the inside. You cannot see the character being formed in a long season while you are in it. That does not mean nothing is happening. The winter does its work underground before anything is visible above.
The End Is Not Ambiguous
Revelation 21:4
"He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away."
This is not a metaphor. It is a promise about the actual future of the actual world. Whatever this season contains — the body that isn't working, the thing that isn't resolving, the grief that isn't lifting — it has an end, and the end is this. Hold onto the ending when the middle is unbearable.
He Does Not Change
Lamentations 3:22–23
"The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness."
This was written by Jeremiah after Jerusalem was destroyed. Not in the easy season. In the middle of the worst thing that had happened to his people. The "new every morning" was written by a man who had watched everything fall. The faithfulness was not what he felt — it was what he chose to hold onto when everything else had gone.

The Anchor
"We have this as a sure and steadfast anchor of the soul, a hope that enters into the inner place behind the curtain."
Hebrews 6:19 · ESV
An anchor does not feel like security — it feels like weight and restraint. The value of an anchor is not how it feels when you are floating in calm water. It is what it does when the storm comes and everything in you wants to drift. You are not drifting. You are anchored. Even when you cannot feel it.
The Honest Tab
The Slow Drift
What happens when a depleted man reaches for things that offer relief but cost more than they give — and how to stay tethered when the pull is strong

This Is the Tab That Needed to Be Here

Long seasons create conditions where escape is particularly appealing. When a man is exhausted and his nervous system is chronically taxed — by illness, by chronic pain, by the relentlessness of hard circumstances — his brain starts looking for relief. This is not a moral failure. It is biology operating under sustained stress. The problem is not the desire for relief; it is that the things the depleted brain reaches for most readily are often the things that provide the least actual relief and create the most actual damage.

Pornography and sexual sin have a particular pull in hard seasons — not because a man has lost his character, but because they offer something that the depleted man desperately needs: a moment of feeling good that he has full control over, that requires nothing from anyone, that doesn't ask anything of him, and that will reliably deliver a sensation when almost nothing else in his life feels reliable. The offer is real. The cost is also real. And the cost tends to be paid by the relationships and the internal life that are already under the most pressure.

What the Escape Costs

Not shame as punishment — shame is not the goal here. But clarity: the man who uses pornography during a long hard season is adding a layer of isolation, secrecy, and spiritual damage to a life that is already carrying too much. It doesn't relieve the depleted state — it deepens it. And it makes the God who is the actual answer feel further away, not closer.

The Mechanics of Drift in a Hard Season

Drift in a long season rarely looks like a dramatic decision. It looks like small movements, each individually defensible, that accumulate over time into a position far from where the man intended to be.

The Prayer Life Goes Quiet First
Not a decision to stop praying — just a gradual reduction. Too tired in the morning. Too distracted at night. The conversations with God that used to be regular become occasional, then rare. The man doesn't notice the silence has grown until it has been there for weeks. This is usually the first indicator that drift is happening.
Isolation Feels Easier Than Community
Being with people requires things — energy, attention, the performance of being okay when you're not, or the vulnerability of admitting you're not. Both are exhausting. Staying home, staying on the phone, staying in the shallow end of every interaction feels like self-preservation. It is actually acceleration of the drift.
Escape Becomes the Primary Coping Mechanism
Whatever the escape is — screens, food, sexual release, substances, obsessive productivity — it starts small and becomes large. The man knows it isn't actually helping. He does it anyway because he needs something to work, and this is the thing that consistently delivers a sensation. The escalation is gradual. By the time it's a problem, it has been building for a while.
The Internal Monologue Gets Dark
"This is never going to get better." "I am not the man I thought I was." "God is not actually doing anything." These thoughts arrive quietly and get repeated until they start to feel like conclusions rather than fears. The man stops challenging them because he is too tired, and because part of him has started to believe them.

What Tethers You

The answer to drift is not heroic willpower applied to each individual temptation. It is staying connected to the things that tether — the practices and people that keep orienting you back toward God and back toward who you are, even when the feelings are not cooperating.

One Honest Person
Not a support group — one person who knows what is actually happening. Not the managed version, the real one. The pull toward pornography. The numbness in prayer. The fear that this is permanent. One person who gets the real story and will ask you about it. This is not optional in a long season. Isolation is the enemy's primary tool. Community is the primary antidote.
If you don't have this person: name who is closest to being that person in your life, and take one step toward a more honest conversation with them.
Minimum Viable Prayer
Not the full devotional practice you had when you had more capacity. The smallest honest version of turning toward God that you can sustain. A few minutes in the morning with nothing but the honest state of your soul offered to him. The lament structure on the previous tab. One psalm read slowly. The goal is not impressive — the goal is connected. Stay in contact even when the contact feels thin.
What is the smallest version of a prayer practice you could actually do today, given your real capacity right now?
Naming the Pull Before Acting on It
The pull toward escape has more power when it is unnamed. When the urge toward pornography or the numbing behavior arrives — before acting on it, name it out loud or in writing: "I am feeling depleted and I want to escape right now." The naming creates a small amount of space between the impulse and the action. That space is where the choice lives.
You are not trying to make the urge disappear. You are trying to make the choice conscious rather than automatic.
The Body Needs What It Needs
Sleep, food, sunlight, movement — these are not luxuries. They are the conditions that make everything else possible. A man who is chronically sleep-deprived and isolated and physically sedentary is fighting every spiritual battle at a significant disadvantage. Taking care of the body in a long season is not indulgence. It is stewardship of the instrument God is going to use to get you through this.
What is the most neglected physical need right now? What would it take to address it this week?

The Smallest Faithful Step
Just Today
You don't have to get through the whole season today. You only have to be faithful with today. Here is what that can look like when you have very little.

The Problem with the Long View

One of the specific difficulties of a long season is that it is long — and looking at the whole of it at once is paralyzing. If you try to hold the full weight of everything that has been hard and everything that might still be ahead, the load is crushing. The men who do not make it through long seasons are often not undone by any single hard day; they are undone by trying to carry all the days at once.

Jesus addressed this directly: "Do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow will worry about itself. Each day has enough trouble of its own" (Matthew 6:34). This is not advice to be irresponsible about the future. It is a prescription for the specific psychological and spiritual danger of carrying tomorrow's weight today. You cannot be faithful to tomorrow today. You can only be faithful to today.

"This is the day that the Lord has made; let us rejoice and be glad in it."
Psalm 118:24 · ESV

This verse is often used as a morning affirmation — a call to enthusiasm. In context, it was written by a man who had been severely afflicted, pushed to the point of falling, and surrounded by enemies. "This is the day that the Lord has made" was not the statement of a man whose circumstances were good. It was the statement of a man who had decided that this day — this specific hard day — was still the Lord's, and that was enough to stand on.

What Faithfulness Looks Like With Very Little

In a long season, faithfulness does not look like what it looks like when you have full capacity. That is not failure — it is calibration to reality. Here is what faithfulness can look like when your tank is mostly empty:

Show Up Without Performing
Go to the gathering, the prayer, the meal — without requiring yourself to feel it or produce it. Presence is faithfulness. You are not responsible for your emotional state in worship; you are responsible for showing up. Let God do what he does with your presence. Your job is to be there.
Receive Without Earning
In a depleted season, you will need to receive things — help, care, grace, rest — that you cannot reciprocate immediately. This is very hard for men who define themselves by what they give. The long season is often the season where God is teaching a man to receive. Let people care for you. Let the community carry some of your weight. This is not weakness; it is the Body of Christ functioning as it was designed to.
One Small Obedience
Not the full practice. Not the recovered version of your spiritual life. One small, specific obedience today — one honest prayer, one honest conversation, one choice not to drift, one moment of gratitude for something specific. The small obedience is not less than the big one. In a depleted season, it may require more.
Name One Good Thing
Not toxic positivity — not pretending the hard things aren't hard. But once a day, name one specific thing that is genuinely good. A person. A moment. The fact that you are still here and still trying. Not to cancel out the grief, but to tell the truth about the whole of the day. Gratitude and grief can coexist. Both are honest. Both are allowed.
Take the Break Without Guilt
If your body or brain requires rest — take it. Not as defeat, not as spiritual failure, not with the commentary that you should be able to do more. As stewardship. A man who destroys his capacity fighting the long season will not be available for what comes on the other side of it. Rest is not quitting. In a long season, rest is often the most faithful thing available.
End the Day With the Doxology
Not necessarily the words — but the posture. At the end of the day, however it went, offer it back to God. The good parts and the failed parts and the parts you are not sure about. "This day was yours. I did what I could. I am giving it back to you." Then sleep. Tomorrow is not your problem yet.

For the Moment When You Have Nothing Left

There will be moments in a long season — maybe today, maybe tonight — when you genuinely have nothing. No words for prayer. No capacity for effort. No emotional resource to draw on. For that moment, here is the smallest faithful thing:

The Smallest Prayer

"I have nothing. I am here. You are God."

That is a complete prayer. It has address, honest complaint, and trust — compressed into three sentences for a man who cannot produce more. It is enough. The Spirit intercedes for us "with groanings too deep for words" (Romans 8:26). You do not have to find the words. You have to show up. God will do the rest.

The Promise for the Long Season
"He gives power to the faint, and to him who has no might he increases strength. They who wait for the Lord shall renew their strength; they shall mount up with wings like eagles; they shall run and not be weary; they shall walk and not faint."
Isaiah 40:29, 31 · ESV
Notice the sequence: eagles, then running, then walking. Most people read it as an escalation — wings, then running, then even walking. But in the context of a long season, the most costly faithfulness is the last one. Eagles soar on adrenaline. Running is the crisis response. Walking is the long middle — one foot in front of the other, for a long time, without the adrenaline. He promises strength for that too. Even for walking. Even for just today.
